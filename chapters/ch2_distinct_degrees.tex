\section{Subgraphs with Many Degrees}
\label{sec:ch2}

In this chapter, we present the following result of Bukh and Sudakov.

\begin{theorem}[Bukh--Sudakov {\cite{BUKH2007612}}]
  \label{thm:bs2007}
  Every Ramsey graph on $n$ vertices induces a subgraph on $\Omega(n)$ vertices with $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ distinct degrees.
\end{theorem}

This was originally conjectured by Erd\H{o}s, Faudree, and S\'{o}s \cite{EFS} in light of the observation that the random graph $G_{n, 1/2}$ exhibits such a property almost surely. In particular, this theorem already has implications for the edge statistics of Ramsey graphs $G$, as the subgraphs $G - \{v\}$ for $v \in V(G)$ have $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ different sizes. This construction lays the foundation for the proof in the next chapter.

\subsection{Diversity}

To prove Theorem \ref{thm:bs2007}, Bukh and Sudakov introduced the notion of \textit{diversity}, which ensures that the neighbourhoods of most pairs of vertices are not too similar.

\begin{definition}
  \label{def:diversity}

  An $n$-vertex graph $G$ is \textit{$(\eps, \delta)$-diverse} if for each $x \in V(G)$, we have $|N(x) \triangle N(y)| \geq \eps n$ for all but $\leq n^{\delta}$ vertices $y \in V(G)$.
\end{definition}

Notice that $(\eps, \delta)$-diversity directly conditions our assumption that $|N(x) \triangle N(y)| = D \geq \eps n$ in the anticoncentration principle discussion in Section \ref{sec:anticoncentration}. In particular, we may show in a diverse graph that $\prob[\deg_U(x) = \deg_U(y)] = O(1/\sqrt{n})$ for all but $\leq n^{\delta}$ pairs of vertices $x, y \in V(G)$, from which we use Turan's theorem to conclude that there are many distinct degrees in a diverse graph. The following lemma formalizes this observation and reduces Theorem \ref{thm:bs2007} to showing every Ramsey graph contains a diverse subgraph of linear size.

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lem:diversity-degrees}

  Let $\delta \in (0, 1/2)$ and let $G$ be an $(\eps, \delta)$-diverse graph on $n$ vertices for large enough $n$. Then there is a subgraph on $\geq n/4$ vertices containing vertices of $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ distinct degrees.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  Let $U$ be a uniformly random subset of $V(G)$. It suffices to show that with positive probability, $|U| \geq n/4$ and $G[U]$ contains $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ vertices of distinct degrees. 

  For distinct $x, y \in V(G)$, define $\mathbbm{1}_{xy}$ to be the indicator for the event that $x, y \in U$ and $\deg_U(x) = \deg_U(y)$. Then $\E[\mathbbm{1}_{xy}] \leq \prob[\deg_U(x) = \deg_U(y)]$. Put $D = N_G(x) \triangle N_G(y), A = N_G(x) \setminus N_G(y)$, and $B = N_G(y) \setminus N_G(x)$. Then the random variable
  \[
    \deg_U(x) - \deg_U(y) = |A \cap U| - |B \cap U| = \sum_{v \in A} \mathbbm{1}_{v \in U} - \sum_{v \in B} \mathbbm{1}_{v \in U}
  \]
  is of $(|D|, 1/2)$-Littlewood--Offord type. Thus by Littlewood--Offord theorem (Proposition \ref{prop:littlewood-offord}),
  \[
    \prob\bigl[\deg_U(x) = \deg_U(y)\bigr] = O\bigl(1/\sqrt{|D|}\bigr).
  \]
  In particular, $\E[\mathbbm{1}_{xy}] = O(1/\sqrt{n})$, whenever $|D| \geq \eps n$.

  Now consider the degree collision graph $H$ on $U$, where $x, y \in U$ are adjacent if and only if $\deg_U(x) = \deg_U(y)$. Since $G$ is $(\eps, \delta)$-diverse, $\E[\mathbbm{1}_{xy}] = O(1/\sqrt{n})$ for all but $\leq n^{1 + \delta} < n^{3/2}$ pairs $x, y \in V(G)$. Hence, the expected number of edges in $H$ is
  \[
    \E[e(H)] = \sum_{\{x, y\} \in V(G)^2} \E[\mathbbm{1}_{xy}] \leq n^{1 + \delta} + n^2 \cdot O\bigl(1/\sqrt{n}\bigr) = O\bigl(n^{3/2}\bigr)
  \] 
  and Markov's inequality yields $\prob[e(H) = O(n^{3/2})] \geq 1/2$. Since $|U| < n/4$ with probability $o(1)$ by the Chernoff bound, the union bound yields a positive probability that $|U| \geq n/4$ and $e(H) \leq O(n^{3/2})$ occur simultaneously, in which case Turan's Theorem (Proposition \ref{prop:turan}) gives an independent set $I$ in $H$ of size at least 
  \[
    |I| \geq \frac{|U|^2}{2e(H) + |U|} = \frac{(n/4)^2}{O(n^{3/2}) + n/4} = \Omega\bigl(\sqrt{n}\bigr).
  \]
  In particular, each vertex in $I$ has a distinct degree in $G[U]$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Richness}

It remains to show that every Ramsey graph contains a diverse subgraph of linear size. Instead of directly proving this, we prove the same statement on a strengthening of diversity called \textit{richness}.

\begin{definition}
  \label{def:richness}
  An $n$-vertex graph $G$ is \textit{$(\eps, \delta)$-rich} if, for $W \subseteq V(G)$ with $|W| \geq \delta n$, we have $|N(v) \cap W| \geq \eps|W|$ and $|\overline{N(v)} \cap W| \geq \eps|W|$ for all but $\leq n^{\delta}$ vertices $v \in V(G)$.
\end{definition}

In other words, almost every vertex $v \in V(G)$ satisfies 
\[
  \eps |W| \leq |N(v) \cap W| \leq (1 - \eps) |W|,
\]
whenever $|W|$ is large. Thus, richness ensures neighborhoods are not unusually concentrated in nor absent from large sets, which is exactly the density control one has in random graphs like $G_{n, 1/2}$, where $|N(v) \cap W| \approx |W|/2$ by the Chernoff bound. When $W$ is the neighborhood of another vertex, richness ensures both sides of the symmetric difference $|N(x) \triangle N(y)| = |N(x) \cap \overline{N(y)}| + |\overline{N(x)} \cap N(y)|$ are large, rather than merely the symmetric difference itself being large. This is why richness is stronger than diversity.

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lem:richness-implies-diversity}
  Let an $n$-vertex graph $G$ be $(\eps, \delta)$-rich, with $\delta \leq 1/2$. Then $G$ is $(\eps/2, \delta)$-diverse.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  Note that for each $x \in V(G)$, either $|N(x)| \geq n/2$ or $|\overline{N(x)}| \geq n/2$. If $|N(x)| \geq n/2$, then richness implies that
  \[
    |\overline{N(y)} \cap N(x)| \geq \eps |N(x)| \geq \eps n/2
  \]
  for all but $\leq n^{\delta}$ vertices $y \in V(G)$. Similarly, if $|\overline{N(x)}| \geq n/2$, then
  \[
    |N(y) \cap \overline{N(x)}| \geq \eps |\overline{N(x)}| \geq \eps n/2
  \]
  for all but $\leq n^{\delta}$ vertices $y \in V(G)$. But then in either case,
  \[
    |N(x) \triangle N(y)| = |\overline{N(y)} \cap N(x)|  + |N(y) \cap \overline{N(x)}| \geq \eps n/2.
  \]
\end{proof}

Richness also implies the reversed notion of diversity.

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lem:richness-implies-upper-bound}
  Let an $n$-vertex graph $G$ be $(\eps, \delta)$-rich. Then $G$ has $\leq n^{1 + \delta}$ pairs of vertices $x_1, x_2 \in V(G)$ with $|N(x_1) \triangle \overline{N(x_2)}| < (\eps/2)n$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  We show that for each choice of $x_1 \in V(G)$, there are $\leq n^{\delta}$ vertices $x_2 \in V(G)$ with $|N(x_1) \triangle \overline{N(x_2)}| < (\eps/2)n$. Since either $|N(x_1)| \geq n/2$ or $|\overline{N(x_1)}| \geq n/2$, we assume without loss of generality that $|N(x_1)| \geq n/2$. By $(\eps, \delta)$-richness, there are $\leq n^{\delta}$ vertices $x_2$ with $|N(x_2) \cap N(x_1)| < \eps n/2$. For all other choices of $x_2$, 
  \[
    |N(x_1) \triangle \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq |N(x_1) \cap N(x_2)|  \geq (\eps/2) n.
  \]
  The result now follows from the fact that there are only $n$ choices of $x_1$.
\end{proof}

\textit{Remark:} Since $|N(x_1) \triangle N(x_2)| + |N(x_1) \triangle \overline{N(x_2)}| = n$ for any two vertices $x_1, x_2$, this lemma gives an upper bound for the symmetric difference
\[
  |N(x_1) \triangle N(x_2)| \leq (1 - \eps/2)n
\]  
for most pairs of vertices $x_1, x_2$.

We now show that every Ramsey graph contains a rich subgraph of linear size. Specifically, we show that if this is not the case, we may construct a highly sparse/dense subset $S \subseteq V(G)$ via induction, and invoke the Erd\H{o}s--Szemer\'{e}di theorem to find a large homogeneous set in $G[S]$.

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lem:rich-subgraph}

  For $\delta \in (0, 1)$, there exists $\eps = \eps(C) > 0$ such that every $C$-Ramsey graph $G$ on $n$ vertices contains an $(\eps, \delta)$-rich subgraph on $\Omega(n)$ vertices.
\end{lemma}

\textit{Remark:} $\eps = \eps(C)$ is independent of $\delta$, so we may take $\delta$ arbitrarily small for any given $\eps$.

\begin{proof}
  Suppose for contradiction that every set of $\geq cn$ vertices fails to induce a $(\eps, \delta)$-rich subgraph, for small $\eps = \eps(C)$ and $c = (\delta/4)^{K}$ with large $K = K(C, \delta)$ to be determined. We inductively construct a sequence of disjoint vertex sets $S_1, S_2, \ldots, S_K$ of size $s = (cn)^\delta/2$, such that for all $S_i$ one of the following conditions holds:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item[(i)] $d(S_i, S_j) < 4\eps$ for $j > i$,
    \item[(ii)] $d(S_i, S_j) > 1 - 4\eps$ for $j > i$.
  \end{enumerate}
  That is, each $S_i$ is small and extremely sprase or dense between each other. Alongside, we also construct a sequence of induced subgraphs $G = G[U_0] \supset G[U_1] \supset \cdots \supset G[U_K]$, such that $|U_i| \geq (\delta/4)|U_{i - 1}|$ and $S_i \subseteq U_{i - 1}$ for all $i$.

  We first show how this construction gives the lemma. By working with $\overline{G}$ instead of $G$ if necessary, we may assume that $r \geq K/2$ of the sets $S_{1}, \ldots, S_{K}$ satisfy condition (i), say $S_{i_1}, \ldots, S_{i_r}$. Put $S = \bigsqcup_{k = 1}^r S_{i_k}$. Since each $S_{i_k}$ has size $s$,
  \[
    d(S) = \frac{\sum_{k = 1}^r e(S_{i_k})}{\binom{|S|}{2}} + \frac{\sum_{k < l} e(S_{i_k}, S_{i_l})}{\binom{|S|}{2}} \leq \frac{r\binom{s}{2}}{\binom{rs}{2}} + \max_{(k, l)} d(S_{i_k}, S_{i_l}) \leq \frac{1}{r} + 4\eps \leq \frac{2}{K} + 4\eps.
  \]
  Set $d = \frac{2}{K} + 4\eps$ and note that it can be taken arbitrarily small. The Erd\H{o}s--Szemer\'{e}di theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:ramsey-density}) now yields a homogeneous set $A \subseteq S$ of size
  \[
    |A| \geq \frac{\gamma d}{\log(1/d)} \log |S| = \frac{\gamma \delta d}{\log(1/d)} \log cn = \frac{\gamma \delta d}{\log(1/d)} \log n + o(\log n),
  \]
  for some absolute constant $\gamma > 0$ given by the theorem. Take $d$ to be small enough so that $\frac{\gamma \delta d}{\log(1/d)} > C$. Then $|A| > C\log n$, contradiction.

  We now construct the sets $S_i$ and $U_i$ inductively. Suppose $U_1, \ldots, U_{i - 1}$ and $S_1, \ldots, S_{i - 1}$ have been constructed. By induction, $|U_{i - 1}| \geq (\delta/4)^{i - 1}|U_0| \geq cn$. Since $G[U_{i - 1}]$ is not $(\eps, \delta)$-rich by assumption, $U_{i - 1}$ contains subsets $W_i, Y_i$ with $|W_i| \geq \delta|U_{i - 1}|$ and $|Y_i| = (cn)^{\delta}$, such that for each $y \in Y_i$ we have $|N_{G_{i - 1}}(y) \cap W_i| < \eps|W_i|$ or $|\overline{N_{G_{i - 1}}(y)} \cap W_i| < \eps|W_i|$. We consider two cases.

  Suppose $|N_{G_{i - 1}}(y) \cap W_i| < \eps|W_i|$ for half the vertices $y \in Y_i$, and let $S_i$ be the corresponding subset of $Y_i$, so $|S_i| = (cn)^{\delta}/2$. Put $T_i = W_i \setminus S_i$ and note that $|T_i| \geq |W_i|/2$, as $|S_i| \leq |W_i|/2$ for large enough $n$. Then for $y \in S_i$,
  \[
    d(y, T_i) = \frac{|N_{G_{i - 1}}(y) \cap T_i|}{|T_i|} \leq \frac{|N_{G_{i - 1}}(y) \cap W_i|}{|T_i|} < \frac{\eps |W_i|}{|W_i|/2} = 2\eps.
  \]
  Partition $T_i$ into $U_i \sqcup Z_i$ where $U_i = \{v \in T_i : d(v, S_i) < 4 \eps\}$. We show that $|U_i| \geq (\delta/4)|U_{i - 1}|$ by double counting $e(T_i, S_i)/|S_i|$. Since $d(v, S_i) \geq 4\eps$ for $v \in Z_i$,
  \[
    \frac{e(T_i, S_i)}{|S_i|} = \sum_{v \in T_i} d(v, S_i) \geq \sum_{v \in Z_i} d(v, S_i) \geq 4\eps|Z_i|.
  \]
  On the other hand, since $d(y, T_i) < 2\eps$ for $y \in S_i$,
  \[
    \frac{e(T_i, S_i)}{|S_i|} = \frac{1}{|S_i|}\sum_{y \in S_i} e(y, T_i) = \frac{|T_i|}{|S_i|}\sum_{y \in S_i} d(y, T_i) < 2\eps|T_i|.
  \]
  Combining both inequalities yields $|Z_i| < |T_i|/2$. Therefore
  \[
    |U_i| = |T_i| - |Z_i| > \frac{|T_i|}{2} \geq \frac{|W_i|}{4} \geq \frac{\delta}{4}|U_{i - 1}|.
  \]
  For $j > i$, since $S_j \subseteq U_{j - 1} \subseteq U_i$, we have $d(v, S_i) < 4\eps$ for every $v \in S_j$, and averaging gives $d(S_i, S_j) < 4\eps$. Thus $S_i$ satisfies condition~(i).

  Otherwise, $|\overline{N_{G_{i - 1}}(y)} \cap W_i| < \eps|W_i|$ for half the vertices $y \in Y_i$. Let $S_i$ be the corresponding subset of $Y_i$ and put $T_i = W_i \setminus S_i$, so again $|T_i| \geq |W_i|/2$ and $|S_i| = (cn)^{\delta}/2$. For $y \in S_i$,
  \[
    1 - d(y, T_i) = \frac{|\overline{N_{G_{i - 1}}(y)} \cap T_i|}{|T_i|} \leq \frac{|\overline{N_{G_{i - 1}}(y)} \cap W_i|}{|T_i|} < \frac{\eps |W_i|}{|W_i|/2} = 2\eps,
  \]
  so $d(y, T_i) > 1 - 2\eps$ for every $y \in S_i$. Partition $T_i$ into $U_i \sqcup Z_i$ where $U_i = \{v \in T_i : d(v, S_i) > 1 - 4\eps\}$. We double count $|T_i| - e(T_i, S_i)/|S_i| = \sum_{v \in T_i}(1 - d(v, S_i))$. Since $1 - d(v, S_i) \geq 4\eps$ for $v \in Z_i$,
  \[
    |T_i| - \frac{e(T_i, S_i)}{|S_i|} = \sum_{v \in T_i}\bigl(1 - d(v, S_i)\bigr) \geq \sum_{v \in Z_i}\bigl(1 - d(v, S_i)\bigr) \geq 4\eps|Z_i|.
  \]
  On the other hand, using $d(y, T_i) > 1 - 2\eps$ for $y \in S_i$,
  \[
    |T_i| - \frac{e(T_i, S_i)}{|S_i|} = |T_i| - \frac{|T_i|}{|S_i|}\sum_{y \in S_i} d(y, T_i) < |T_i| - (1 - 2\eps)|T_i| = 2\eps|T_i|.
  \]
  Combining both inequalities gives $|Z_i| < |T_i|/2$, hence $|U_i| > |T_i|/2 \geq (\delta/4)|U_{i - 1}|$ as in the other case. For  $j > i$, each $v \in S_j \subseteq U_i$ satisfies $d(v, S_i) > 1 - 4\eps$, so $d(S_i, S_j) > 1 - 4\eps$. Thus $S_i$ satisfies condition~(ii). This completes the induction.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:bs2007}}

Theorem \ref{thm:bs2007} now follows immediately by applying Lemmas \ref{lem:rich-subgraph}, \ref{lem:richness-implies-diversity}, and \ref{lem:diversity-degrees} in sequence.

Let $G$ be a $C$-Ramsey graph on $n$ vertices, and fix any $\delta \in (0, 1/2)$. By Lemma \ref{lem:rich-subgraph}, there exists an $(\eps, \delta)$-rich induced subgraph $G' \subseteq G$ on $m = \Omega(n)$ vertices. By Lemma \ref{lem:richness-implies-diversity}, $G'$ is $(\eps/2, \delta)$-diverse. Applying Lemma \ref{lem:diversity-degrees} to $G'$, we obtain an induced subgraph of $G'$ on $\Omega(m) = \Omega(n)$ vertices containing $\Omega(\sqrt{m}) = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$ vertices of distinct degrees. \qed
