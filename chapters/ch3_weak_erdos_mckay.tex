\section{Subgraphs of Quadratically Many Sizes}
\label{sec:ch3}

Erd\H{o}s and McKay conjectured that for any Ramsey graph $G$ on $n$ vertices, there exists $\delta = \delta(C) > 0$ such that
\[
  \bigl\{1, 2, \ldots, \delta n^2\bigr\} \subseteq \Phi(G) \coloneqq \bigl\{e(H) : H \text{ is an induced subgraph of } G\bigr\}.
\]
That is, for any $1 \leq t \leq \delta n^2$, there exists an induced subgraph with exactly $t$ edges. The conjecture was widely open for decades. In 2003, Alon, Krivelevich and Sudakov \cite{AlonKrivelevichSudakov2003} proved the conjecture with $n^{\delta}$ in place of $\delta n^2$.
\begin{theorem}[Alon, Krivelevich and Sudakov {\cite{AlonKrivelevichSudakov2003}}]
  \label{thm:alks2003}
  For any $C > 0$, there exists $\delta = \delta(C) > 0$ such that
  \[
  \bigl\{1, 2, \ldots, n^{\delta}\bigr\} \subseteq \Phi(G).
  \]
\end{theorem}
Since the conjecture implies $|\Phi(G)| = \Omega(n^2)$ and trivially $|\Phi(G)| \leq \binom{n}{2}$, a natural relaxation is to ask whether $\Phi(G)$ indeed has quadratic size. In 2017, Narayanan, Sahasrabudhe, and Tomon \cite{narayanan2017ramseygraphsinducesubgraphs} proved a near optimal bound of $|\Phi(G)| = n^{2 - o(1)}$ by building on the notion of diversity from Chapter~\ref{sec:ch2}. The desired quadratic bound was achieved by Kwan and Sudakov \cite{kwan2021ramseygraphsinducesubgraphs} a few years later, and this chapter is dedicated to presenting their proof.

\begin{theorem}[Kwan and Sudakov {\cite{kwan2021ramseygraphsinducesubgraphs}}]
  \label{thm:ks}
  For any $C > 0$, 
  \[
    |\Phi(G)| = \Theta(n^2).
  \]
\end{theorem}

Recall that Theorem \ref{thm:bs2007} provides a bound of $|\Phi(G)| = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$ for any Ramsey graph $G$ by removing a single vertex to construct each subgraph. Two ideas may be used to improve this construction. First, we apply the same idea to many different induced subgraphs rather than merely $G$ itself. Second, instead of deleting only one vertex, we add and remove various combinations of vertices to produce many more distinct sizes. 

Specifically, pick a target $m$ and find two disjoint vertex sets $U$ and $W$, where $U$ is a linear-sized random set with $e(U) \approx m$ and $W$ is an augmenting set of size $O(\sqrt{n})$ selected from a \textit{candidate set} $W_0 \subseteq V(G) \setminus U$ whose degrees are of linear order and all lie in a narrow interval. Then the subsets $Z \subseteq W$ produce $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ distinct values of $e(U \sqcup Z)$ that all lie in an interval of length $O(n^{3/2})$ centred at $e(U)$. Applying this to $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ well-separated targets $m$ yields the full $\Omega(n^2)$ count.

Kwan and Sudakov construct $W$ as three disjoint parts $\mathbf{S}$, $\mathbf{T}$, $\mathbf{X}$, each of size $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$, that act at complementary scales. Every element has $\Theta(n)$ edges into $U$, and the degrees from $\mathbf{S}$ into $U$ are uniformly smaller than those from $\mathbf{T}$ by a gap of $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$. Three operation on $Z \subseteq W$ shift $e(U \sqcup Z)$ at three separate scales. Varying $k = |Z \cap (\mathbf{S} \sqcup \mathbf{T})|$ shifts $e(U \sqcup Z)$ at the coarse scale $\Theta(n)$. With $k$ fixed, exchanging an element of $\mathbf{S}$ for an element of $\mathbf{T}$ shifts $e(U \sqcup Z)$ by $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$, so the achievable values at this level are spaced by $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$. The set $\mathbf{X}$ will contain $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ distinct degrees into $U$, so varying the singleton $x \in \mathbf{X}$ fills these $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ gaps. Hence, the different combinations of $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$ yield $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ distinct values of $e(U \sqcup Z)$.

Achieving the requirement for $\mathbf{X}$ is the main technical challenge, as vertex diversity defined in Chapter~\ref{sec:ch2} does not produce enough distinct degrees in this case. In the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:diversity-degrees}, we saw that $\prob[\deg_U(x) = \deg_U(y)] = O(1/\sqrt{n})$ for all but $\leq n^{\delta}$ pairs $x, y \in V(G)$ in an $(\eps, \delta)$-diverse graph, and the same construction yields the collision graph $H$ on the candidate set $W_0$ with expected number of edges
\[
  \E[e(H)] = O\bigl(|W_0|^2/\sqrt{n} + |W_0| \cdot n^{\delta}\bigr),
\]
producing an independent set $I \subseteq W_0$ of size 
\[
  |I| = \frac{|W_0|^2}{2\E[e(H)] + |W_0|} = \Omega\left(\sqrt{n} \cdot \frac{|W_0|}{|W_0| + n^{1/2 + \delta}}\right).
\]
by Tur\'{a}n's theorem. We thus need $|W_0| = \Omega(n^{1/2 + \delta})$ for $|I|$ to reach $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$. Unfortunately, $W_0$ is extracted by pigeonholing $n$ vertices into $O(\sqrt{n})$ degree intervals of length $O(\sqrt{n})$, so at best $|W_0| = \Theta(\sqrt{n})$, yielding only $|I| = \Omega(n^{1/2 - \delta})$.

To overcome this obstacle, Kwan and Sudakov's construction allows the candidate set $W_0$ to also contain disjoint \textit{pairs} while treating them like singletons. Write $\mathbf{x}$ in bold to denote an object that is either a single vertex or a pair of vertices, and similarly for sets of such objects. For $\mathbf{x} = \{x_1, x_2\}$, define $N(\mathbf{x}) \coloneqq N(x_1) \cup N(x_2)$ as a multiset union and $\deg(\mathbf{x}) \coloneqq |N(\mathbf{x})| = \deg(x_1) + \deg(x_2)$ as the \textit{pair-degree}. Note that there are $\binom{n}{2} = \Theta(n^2)$ pairs, but the pair-degrees take only $O(n)$ values. Pigeonholing now yields $\Theta(n)$ disjoint pairs sharing a common pair-degree, vastly exceeding the $n^{1/2 + \delta}$ threshold. The price is that we need the symmetric difference $|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$ not just for most pairs of vertices, but for most pairs of \emph{pairs}. We call this \textit{pair-diversity}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:pair_diverse}
  An $n$-vertex graph $G$ is \textit{$(\eps, \delta, \alpha)_2$-diverse} if for each pair of vertices $\mathbf{x} = \{x_1, x_2\}$ in $G$ with $|N(x_1) \triangle \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq \alpha n$, there are $\leq n^{\delta}$ mutually disjoint pairs of vertices $\mathbf{y} = \{y_1, y_2\}$ with $\mathbf{x} \cap \mathbf{y} = \emptyset$, such that $|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})| < \eps n$.
\end{definition}

It turns out that richness also implies pair-diversity.

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lem:richness-implies-pair-diversity}
  Let an $n$-vertex graph $G$ be $(\eps, \delta)$-rich, with $\delta \leq 1/2$. Then $G$ is $(\alpha\eps/2, \delta, \alpha)_2$-diverse for any $\alpha \geq 2\delta$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  Suppose not. Then there exists a pair $\mathbf{x} = \{x_1, x_2\}$ with $|N(x_1) \triangle \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq \alpha n$ and a set $Y$ of $n^{\delta}$ pairs with $|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})| < \alpha \eps n /2$ for all $\mathbf{y} \in Y$. Since
  \[
    |N(x_1) \triangle \overline{N(x_2)}| = |N(x_1) \cap N(x_2)|  + |\overline{N(x_1)} \cap \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq \alpha n,
  \]
  we have $|N(x_1) \cap N(x_2)| \geq (\alpha/2)n \geq \delta n$ or $|\overline{N(x_1)} \cap \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq (\alpha/2)n \geq \delta n$. Assume without loss of generality that the former case holds. Then for each vertex $y$ in any $\mathbf{y} \in Y$, 
  \[
    \left|\overline{N(y)} \cap \bigl(N(x_1) \cap N(x_2)\bigr)\right| \leq |N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})| < \alpha \eps n /2 \leq \eps |N(x_1) \cap N(x_2)|.
  \]
  But then the set of all such $y$ now contradicts the $(\eps, \delta)$-rich condition.
\end{proof}

One last thing we need is to ensure $\mathbf{X}$ actually provides $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ distinct degrees into $U \sqcup \mathbf{Z} \coloneqq U \sqcup \bigsqcup_{\mathbf{z} \in \mathbf{Z}} \mathbf{z}$ for each choice of augmenting set $\mathbf{Z} \subseteq \mathbf{S} \sqcup \mathbf{T}$. Notice the current construction of $\mathbf{X}$ only ensures distinct degrees $\deg_U(\mathbf{x})$ into $U$ instead of $\deg_{U \sqcup Z}(\mathbf{x})$. If we sampled $U$ once and directly constructed $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$ based on $U$, then the $\deg_U(\mathbf{x})$ would be fixed numbers given the outcome of $U$. However, there is no guarantee that $\deg_{U \sqcup Z}(\mathbf{x}) = \deg_{U}(\mathbf{x}) + \deg_{Z}(\mathbf{x})$ would remain distinct, as the addition of $\deg_{Z}(\mathbf{x})$ could cancel out the uniqueness of $\deg_{U}(\mathbf{x})$. Kwan and Sudakov overcome this with a \textit{double-exposure} technique. They perform two rounds of sampling. First, they sample the random set $U_0$ and construct $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$ relative to $U_0$. In the second round, they sample $U$ from $U_0$ by independently keeping each vertex with probability $1/2$. After the first sampling, the construction guarantees $|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$ for all $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbf{X}$, and the augmenting sets $\mathbf{Z} \subseteq \mathbf{S} \cup \mathbf{T}$ are fully determined by $U_0$. In the second round, the difference $\deg_U(\mathbf{x}) - \deg_U(\mathbf{y})$ becomes a sum of $\Omega(n)$ independent Bernoulli terms, and Erd\H{o}s--Littlewood--Offord yields $\prob[\deg_U(\mathbf{x}) - \deg_U(\mathbf{y}) = t] = O(1/\sqrt{n})$ for every integer $t$. In particular, since $t_{xy} \coloneqq \deg_Z(\mathbf{y}) - \deg_Z(\mathbf{x})$ is deterministic for each $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$, 
\[
  \prob\bigl[\deg_{U \sqcup Z}(\mathbf{x}) = \deg_{U \sqcup Z}(\mathbf{y})\bigr] = \prob\bigl[\deg_{U}(\mathbf{x}) - \deg_{U}(\mathbf{y}) = t_{xy}\bigr] = O(1/\sqrt{n}).
\]
The collision graph argument now gives the desired set of $U$ such that $\deg_{U \sqcup Z}(\mathbf{x})$ is distinct for $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$.

\subsection{The main lemma}

As outlined above, we will find many induced subgraphs $G[U]$ whose edge counts are well-separated from each other, and show that each can be augmented in $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ ways. Theorem~\ref{thm:ks} will be an immediate consequence of the following lemma, which we dedicate the rest of this chapter to proving.

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts}

  Let $C > 0$. Then there exists $c = c(C) > 0$, such that for any $C$-Ramsey graph $G$ on $n$ vertices and any $m$ satisfying $cn^2 \leq m \leq 2cn^2$, there are disjoint subsets $U, W \subseteq V(G)$ with $|e(U) - m| = O(n^{3/2})$ and $|W| = O(\sqrt{n})$ such that 
  \[
    \left|\bigl\{e(U \sqcup Z) : Z \subseteq W\bigr\}\right| = \Omega(n^{3/2}).
  \]
\end{lemma}

We first show how this implies Theorem \ref{thm:ks}.

\paragraph{Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:ks}.} For any $U, W$ given by Lemma~\ref{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts},
\[
  |e(U \sqcup W) - e(U)| = O(n^{3/2}),
\]
as $|W| = O(\sqrt{n})$ and each $w \in W$ has $\leq n$ neighbours in $U \sqcup W$. But then the $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ values of $e(U \sqcup Z)$ for $Z \subseteq W$ are contained in an interval of length $O(n^{3/2})$ centred at $e(U)$. Partition the interval between $cn^2$ and $2cn^2$ into $\frac{c}{k}\sqrt{n}$ subintervals of length $kn^{3/2}$, for some large constant $k$. Apply Lemma \ref{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts} to a suitable choice of $m$ in each subinterval so that the obtained values of $e(U \sqcup Z)$ for $Z \subseteq W$ lie in the same subinterval as $m$, which is allowed for large enough $k$. Since we applied Lemma \ref{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts} to $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ disjoint subintervals, we obtain $\Omega(n^2)$ distinct sizes of induced subgraphs. \qed

\subsection{The construction of augmenting sets}
\label{sec:construction}

The following lemma constructs $U_0$ and the augmenting sets $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$ with the properties needed for Lemma~\ref{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts}.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:construction}
For any $C > 0$ there exists $c = c(C) > 0$ such that the following holds. For any $C$-Ramsey graph $G$ on $n$ vertices and any $m$ satisfying $cn^2 \leq m \leq 2cn^2$, there is vertex set $U_0 \subseteq V(G)$ with $|e(U_0) - 4m| = O(n^{3/2})$, constant $c' = c'(C) \in (0, 1]$, and sets $\mathbf{S}$, $\mathbf{T}$, $\mathbf{X}$ of size $c'\sqrt{n}$ such that
\begin{enumerate}
  \item either $U_0, \mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X} \subseteq V(G)$ are disjoint sets of vertices, or $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X} \subseteq \binom{V(G)}{2}$ are sets of disjoint pairs of vertices with no vertex appearing in more than one of $U_0, \mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$,
  \item there is $d = \Theta(n)$ such that $\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = d + O(\sqrt{n})$ for each $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{S} \sqcup \mathbf{T} \sqcup \mathbf{X}$,
  \item $\min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{T}} \deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) - \max_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{S}} \deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \geq 8c'\sqrt{n}$,
  \item for each $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{X}}{2}$, we have $|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

Property~2 ensures adding or removing a single element shifts the edge count by $\Theta(n)$. Property~3 guarantees swapping an element of $\mathbf{S}$ for an element of $\mathbf{T}$ shifts the edge count by $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$. Property~4 sets up the Littlewood--Offord argument in the double-exposure step. 

The proof starts with reducing to a rich subgraph using Lemma~\ref{lem:rich-subgraph} and using the pigeonhole principle to obtain a large candidate set $\mathbf{W}_0$ of vertices or pairs with similar degrees, satisfying Property~1 and~2. It then samples $U_0$ at random and verifies that all desired properties hold simultaneously with positive probability.

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}]

Let $\eps = \eps(C)$ be given by Lemma~\ref{lem:rich-subgraph} and set $\delta = \eps/4$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:rich-subgraph}, $G$ contains an $(\eps, \delta)$-rich induced subgraph $G[V']$ on $\Omega(n)$ vertices. Since $\log |V'| \geq \frac{1}{2}\log n$, the graph $G[V']$ is $2C$-Ramsey, so by adjusting constants it suffices to work inside $G[V']$. We may thus assume $G$ itself is $(\eps, \delta)$-rich.

By richness, Lemmas~\ref{lem:richness-implies-diversity}, \ref{lem:richness-implies-upper-bound}, and~\ref{lem:richness-implies-pair-diversity} with $\alpha = \eps/2$ furnish the following properties:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(a)] $G$ is $(\eps/2, \delta)$-diverse,
  \item[(b)] $G$ is $(\eps^2/4, \delta, \eps/2)_2$-diverse,
  \item[(c)] there are $\leq n^{1+\delta}$ pairs
      $\{x_1, x_2\}$ with
      $|N(x_1) \triangle \overline{N(x_2)}| < (\eps/2)n$.
\end{enumerate}

Each of the $\binom{n}{2} = \Theta(n^2)$ vertex pairs $\{x_1, x_2\}$ has a pair-degree $\deg(\{x_1, x_2\}) = \deg(x_1) + \deg(x_2)$ taking values from $0$ to $2n$, so by pigeonhole there exists $d^*$ and a collection $H$ of $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ pairs with $\deg(\mathbf{x}) = d^* + O(\sqrt{n})$ for all $\mathbf{x} \in H$. 

Interpret $H$ as a graph on $V(G)$ whose edges are the $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ pairs. By (c), we may delete $O(n^{1+\delta}) = o(n^{3/2})$ edges in $H$ so that all remaining pairs have $|N(x_1) \triangle \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq (\eps/2)n$. Suppose there exists vertex $v$ in $H$ with degree $\Omega(n^{3/4})$. Take $\mathbf{W}_0 = N_H(v)$ and set $d' = d^* - \deg(v)$. Then each $w \in \mathbf{W}_0$ is a single vertex with degree $\deg(w) = d + O(\sqrt{n})$. Now suppose $H$ has maximum degree $\Delta = o(n^{3/4})$. By iteratively picking an edge and removing $\leq 2\Delta$ edges adjacent to it, we obtain a matching of size $\Omega(n^{3/2})/o(n^{3/4}) = \Omega(n^{3/4})$. Take $\mathbf{W}_0$ to be the pairs in this matching and set $d' = d^*$. Thus, $\mathbf{W}_0$ is now the candidate set, such that $\deg(\mathbf{x}) = d' + O(\sqrt{n})$ for all $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{W}_0$ and all subsets of $\mathbf{W}_0$ satisfy Property~1.

Next, we extract a subset $\mathbf{A} \subseteq \mathbf{W}_0$ of size $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ with pairwise large neighbourhood symmetric differences. Define graph $F$ on $\mathbf{W}_0$ by joining $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$ whenever $|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})| < (\eps^2/4)n$. By diversity, $F$ has $|\mathbf{W}_0| = \Omega(n^{3/4})$ vertices and maximum degree at most $n^{\delta} < n^{1/4}$, so Tur\'{a}n's theorem gives an independent set $\mathbf{A} \subseteq \mathbf{W}_0$ in $F$ of size $\Omega\bigl(|\mathbf{W}_0|/n^{1/4}\bigr) = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$. That is, for $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$, we have $|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$, which implies $d' = \Omega(n)$.

By the Erd\H{o}s--Szemer\'{e}di bound (Corollary~\ref{cor:ramsey-density}), $e(G) \geq 800cn^2$ for some constant $c = c(C) > 0$. For $cn^2 \leq m \leq 2cn^2$, set $p = \sqrt{4m/e(G)}$, so that $p = \Omega(1)$ and $p \leq 0.1$. Let $U_0$ be a random subset of $V(G)$ by including each vertex with probability $p$. 

\begin{claim}
  The following five events each hold with probability greater than $4/5$, and therefore all hold simultaneously with positive probability by the union bound.

  \begin{enumerate}
    \item[(i)] $|e(U_0) - 4m| = O(n^{3/2})$,
    \item[(ii)] There is a set $\mathbf{Q} \subseteq \mathbf{A}$ involving no vertices of $U_0$, with $|\mathbf{Q}| \geq (2/3)|\mathbf{A}|$,
    \item[(iii)] There is a set $\mathbf{R} \subseteq \mathbf{A}$ with $|\mathbf{R}| \geq (2/3)|\mathbf{A}|$ and $\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = pd' + O(\sqrt{n})$ for each $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{R}$,
    \item[(iv)] $|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$ for each $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$,
    \item[(v)] The equality $\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = \deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})$ holds for $O(\sqrt{n})$ pairs $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{claim}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Claim]
  We prove each part in order.
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item[(i)] $\E[e(U_0)] = 4m$ and there are $O(n^3)$ pairs of edges in $G$ whose presence are dependent in $G[U_0]$, as this occurs only if two edges share a vertex. Thus for $e \in E(G)$, the standard variance bound gives
    \[
      \Var\bigl(e(U_0)\bigr) \leq \E[e(U_0)] + O(n^3) = O(n^3).
    \]
    Chebyshev's inequality yields
    \[
      \prob\bigl[|e(U_0) - 4m| = \omega(n^{3/2})\bigr] \leq \frac{O(n^3)}{\omega(n^{3})} \to 0,
    \]
    as $n \to \infty$.
    \item[(ii)] Let $\mathbf{Q}$ be the subset of $\mathbf{A}$ involving no vertices of $U_0$. Then $|\mathbf{Q}|$ follows a binomial distribution with $|\mathbf{A}|$ trials, which has mean at least $(1 - p)^2|\mathbf{A}| \geq 0.8|\mathbf{A}|$ and variance $O(|\mathbf{A}|)$. By Chebyshev's,
    \[
      \prob\left[|\mathbf{Q}| < \frac{2}{3}|\mathbf{A}|\right] \leq \prob\bigl[\E[|\mathbf{Q}|] - |\mathbf{Q}| \geq 0.1|\mathbf{A}|\bigr] \leq \frac{O\bigl(|\mathbf{A}|\bigr)}{0.01|\mathbf{A}|^2} = O\bigl(1/|\mathbf{A}|\bigr) \to 0,
    \]
    as $n \to \infty$. 
    \item[(iii)] For $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{A}$, the degree $\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})$ has mean $pd' + O(\sqrt{n})$ and variance $O(n)$. By Chebyshev's, 
    \begin{align*}
      \prob\bigl[\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = pd' + O(\sqrt{n})\bigr] 
      &\geq 1 - \prob\bigl[|\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) - \E[\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})]| = \omega(\sqrt{n})\bigr] \\
      &\geq 1 - \frac{O(n)}{\omega(n)} \to 1,
    \end{align*}
    In particular, $\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = pd' + O(\sqrt{n})$ with probability $0.99$ for large enough $n$. Let $\mathbf{R} \subseteq \mathbf{A}$ be the set of $\mathbf{x}$ that satisfies this bound. Since $\E[|\mathbf{A} \setminus \mathbf{R}|] = 0.01|\mathbf{A}|,$ Markov's inequality gives
    \[
      \prob\left[|\mathbf{R}| \geq \frac{2}{3}|\mathbf{A}|\right] = 1 - \prob\left[|\mathbf{A} \setminus \mathbf{R}| \geq \frac{1}{3}|\mathbf{A}|\right] \geq 1 - \frac{\E\bigl[|\mathbf{A} \setminus \mathbf{R}|\bigr]}{\frac{1}{3}|\mathbf{A}|} \geq 0.8.
    \]
    \item[(iv)] Fix $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$. Recall that $|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$. Since $|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| = |(N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})) \cap U_0|$ has binomial distribution with parameters $|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})|$ and $p$, the Chernoff bound gives
    \[
      \prob\left[|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| < \frac{p}{2}|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})|\right] = e^{-\Omega(n)} = o(n^{-1}).
    \]
    The result follows from summing over $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$ and invoking the union bound.
    \item[(v)] For $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$, the random variable $\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) - \deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})$ is of $(|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})|, p)$-Littlewood--Offord type. Since $|N(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$, the Littlewood--Offord theorem gives $\prob[\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = \deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})] = O(1/\sqrt{n})$, so the expected number of pairs $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$ satisfying $\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = \deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})$ is $O(\sqrt{n})$. The property now follows from Markov's inequality.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

Fix an outcome of $U_0$ for which all five events hold. Since $|\mathbf{Q} \cap \mathbf{R}| \geq |\mathbf{A}|/3 = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$, the collision graph construction on $\mathbf{Q} \cap \mathbf{R}$ and Tur\'{a}n's theorem yield a set $\mathbf{B}$ of size $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ that has distinct degrees into $U_0$. Partition $\mathbf{B}$ into three equal parts by the degree $\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})$ into $U_0$, and let $\mathbf{T}$ be the top third, $\mathbf{S}$ be the bottom third, and $\mathbf{X}$ be the middle third. Since $\mathbf{B}$ has distinct degrees and $|\mathbf{X}| = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$, we may find constant $c' \in (0, 1]$ that satisfies Property~3. Since $\mathbf{X} \subseteq \mathbf{A}$, Property~4 holds. Set $d = pd'$. Recall that $d' = \Theta(n)$, so $\deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = d + O(\sqrt{n})$ for each $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{S} \sqcup \mathbf{T} \sqcup \mathbf{X}$, satisfying Property~2. Trimming the sizes of $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$ to $c'\sqrt{n}$ completes the proof.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Double-exposure}
\label{sec:double-exposure}

Given $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, c'$ from Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}, fix an ordering on $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{T}$ and define
\[
  \mathcal{P} \coloneqq \bigl\{(k, i) : c'\sqrt{n} \leq k \leq 2c'\sqrt{n} \text{ and } 0 \leq i \leq c'\sqrt{n}\bigr\}.
\]
For $(k, i) \in \mathcal{P}$, define $\mathbf{Z}_{k, i} \subseteq \mathbf{S} \sqcup \mathbf{T}$ by taking the first $k - i$ elements from $\mathbf{S}$ and the first $i$ elements from $\mathbf{T}$. By Properties 2 and 3 of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}, the values of $e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) \coloneqq \sum_{\mathbf{z} \in \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}} \deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{z})$ are evenly spaced by $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ as $(k, i)$ vary lexicographically, which already forms $\Omega(n)$ distinct values of $e(U_0 \sqcup \mathbf{Z}_{k, i})$. It remains to fill the $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ gaps between each $e(U_0 \sqcup \mathbf{Z}_{k, i})$ with $\mathbf{X}$ via the \textit{double-exposure} technique while maintaining the gap structure of $\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}$. Specifically, we sample $U$ from $U_0$ such that the values $e(U \sqcup \mathbf{Z}_{k, i} \sqcup \{\mathbf{x}\})$ are distinct as $\mathbf{Z}_{k, i} \subseteq \mathbf{S} \sqcup \mathbf{T}$ and $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$ vary. Define $U_{k,i} \coloneqq U \sqcup \mathbf{Z}_{k,i}$ and $e_{k, i} \coloneqq e(U_{k, i}) - e(U) = e(\mathbf{Z}_{k,i}) + e(\mathbf{Z}_{k,i}, U)$. We need to ensure the following:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Degree distinctness in $\mathbf{X}$.} For most $(k, i)$, the elements of $\mathbf{X}$ should have $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ pairwise distinct degrees into $U_{k,i}$, all lying in an interval of length $O(\sqrt{n})$. This is the payoff of double-exposure.
    \item \emph{Concentration of the edge count.} The value $e_{k,0}$ should be $O(n)$ around its expectation, so that the $\Theta(n)$ spacings between $e_{k, 0}$ and $e_{k + 1, 0}$ are preserved.
  \item \emph{Regular medium-scale spacing.} For each $k$, the values $e_{k,0}, e_{k,1}, \ldots, e_{k, c'\sqrt{n}}$ should span a range of $\Omega(n)$ and increase roughly uniformly, so that $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ of them are pairwise separated by $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$.
\end{enumerate}

Write $\Delta_{k, i} \coloneqq e_{k, i} - e_{k, i - 1}$, and we have the following lemma, whose first two properties correspond to the first two points above, and whose last two properties combined ensure the regular medium-scale spacing. 

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:double-exposure}
Let $G$ be a $C$-Ramsey graph, let $\mathbf{S}$, $\mathbf{T}$, $\mathbf{X}$, $U_0, c', d$ be obtained from Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}. Let $U$ be a uniformly random subset of $U_0$, and let $U_{k, i}, e_{k, i}, \Delta_{k, i}$ be defined as above. Then there exist positive constants $M, \beta, Q > 0$ depending on $C$, such that $Q \geq 3\beta$ and for each $c'\sqrt{n} \leq k \leq 2c'\sqrt{n}$ the following hold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item With probability $\geq 0.99$, there is a set $\mathcal{I}_k$ of $(1 - \beta/(2M)) \cdot c'\sqrt{n}$ values with the following property: for each $i \in \mathcal{I}_k$, there exists $\mathbf{X}_{k, i} \subseteq \mathbf{X}$ of size $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$, such that the values $\deg_{U_{k,i}}(\mathbf{x})$ for
        $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}_{k,i}$ are distinct and all lie in the interval $[d/2 - Q\sqrt{n},\; d/2 + Q\sqrt{n}]$.

    \item With probability $\geq 0.99$,
    \[
      \bigl|e_{k,0} - \mathbb{E}[e_{k,0}]\bigr| \leq Qn.
    \]

    \item With probability $\geq 1/2$,
    \[
      e_{k,c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k,0} \geq 3\beta n.
    \]

    \item With probability at
        least $0.99$,
        \[
            \sum_{\substack{i \,:\, |\Delta_{k,i}| \geq M\sqrt{n}}} |\Delta_{k,i}| \leq \beta n.
        \]
        That is, the unusually large increments contribute little to the total range $e_{k,c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k,0}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  We prove each property in the order of 3, 4, 1, 2, so that the constants $\beta, M, Q$ are determined in their dependency order.

  \begin{enumerate}
    \item[3.] Note that $|e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})| \leq 2c'\sqrt{n}$, as $|\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}| = k \leq 2c'\sqrt{n}$. This and the construction of $U$ yields
    \begin{align*}
      \E\bigl[e_{k, i} - e_{k, i - 1}\bigr] 
      &= \E\bigl[e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})\bigr] + \bigl[e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})\bigr] \\
      &\geq \frac{1}{2}\bigl[e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})\bigr] - 2c'\sqrt{n}.
    \end{align*}
    Property~3 of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} gives $e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1}) \geq 8c'\sqrt{n}$, so 
    \[
      \E\bigl[e_{k, i} - e_{k, i - 1}\bigr]  \geq 2c'\sqrt{n},
    \]
    for all $i$. Put $\beta = 2(c')^2/3$ so the $e_{k, c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k, 0}$ has expectation $\geq 3\beta n$. Since $e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) = \sum_{u \in U_0} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} \cdot \deg_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u)$,
    \begin{align*}
      e_{k, c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k, 0} 
      &= \bigl[e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, c'\sqrt{n}}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, 0})\bigr] + \bigl[e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, c'\sqrt{n}}) - e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, 0})\bigr] \\
      &= \bigl[e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, c'\sqrt{n}}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, 0})\bigr] + \sum_{u \in U_0} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} \cdot \bigl(\deg_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, c'\sqrt{n}}}(u) - \deg_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, 0}}(u)\bigr)
    \end{align*}
    is of Littlewood--Offord type with parameter $1/2$, which is symmetrically distributed around its expectation. The result now follows.
    \item[4.] Since 
    \[
      \Delta_{k, i} = \bigl[e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})\bigr] + \sum_{u \in U_0} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} \cdot \bigl(\deg_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u) - \deg_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1}}(u)\bigr)
    \]
    is of Littlewood--Offord type and measures the edge count difference of swapping one vertex from $\mathbf{S}$ to $\mathbf{T}$, Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} gives $\E\bigl[\Delta_{k, i}\bigr] = O(\sqrt{n})$ and each $u \in U_0$ contributes at most $|\deg_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u) - \deg_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1}}(u)| \leq 2$ to $\Delta_{k, i}$. Thus by Hoeffding's inequality,
    \[
      \prob[|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq t] \leq 2\exp\left(-\frac{2\left(t - \E\bigl[|\Delta_{k, i}|\bigr]\right)^2}{4|U_0|}\right) = \exp\bigl(-\Omega(t^2/n)\bigr).
    \]
    By the identity $\E[X] = \sum_{t \geq 1} \prob(X \geq t)$ for nonnegative integer random variable $X$, 
    \begin{align*}
      \E\bigl[|\Delta_{k, i}| \cdot \mathbbm{1}_{|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq M\sqrt{n}}\bigr]
      &= \sum_{t = 1}^{\infty} \prob\bigl[|\Delta_{k, i}| \cdot \mathbbm{1}_{|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq M\sqrt{n}} \geq t\bigr] \\
      &= M\sqrt{n} \cdot \prob\bigl[|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq M\sqrt{n}\bigr] + \sum_{t = M\sqrt{n}}^{\infty} \prob\bigl[|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq t\bigr] \\
      &= M\sqrt{n} \cdot \exp\bigl(-\Omega(M^2)\bigr) + \sum_{t = M\sqrt{n}}^{\infty} \exp\bigl(-\Omega(t^2/n)\bigr) \\
      &= \sqrt{n} \cdot \exp\bigl(-\Omega(M^2)\bigr),
    \end{align*}
    uniformly over $M$. For large enough $M$, Markov's inequality now yields
    \[
     \prob\left[\sum_{\substack{i \,:\, |\Delta_{k,i}| \geq M\sqrt{n}}} |\Delta_{k,i}| > \beta n\right] \leq \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathbf{S}|} \E\bigl[|\Delta_{k, i}| \cdot \mathbbm{1}_{|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq M\sqrt{n}}\bigr]}{\beta n} \leq \frac{e^{-\Omega(M^2)}}{\beta} < 0.01.
    \]
    \item[1.] For $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{X}}{2}$, 
    \[
      \deg_{U}(\mathbf{x}) - \deg_{U}(\mathbf{y}) = \sum_{u \in N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} - \sum_{u \in N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} = \sum_{u \in N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})} a_u \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U}
    \]
    with $a_u \in \Z \setminus \{0\}$. Thus for each $k, i$, the random variable $\deg_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x}) - \deg_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{y})$ is of $\bigl(|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})|, 1/2\bigr)$-Littlewood--Offord type, as it is a translation of $\deg_{U}(\mathbf{x}) - \deg_{U}(\mathbf{y})$. By Property~4 of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}, $|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \triangle N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$, so by Erd\H{o}s--Littlewood--Offord,
    \[
      \prob\bigl[\deg_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x}) = \deg_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{y})\bigr] = O(1/\sqrt{n}).
    \]
    Let $H_{k, i}$ be the graph on $\mathbf{X}$ with edges $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{X}}{2}$ such that $\deg_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x}) = \deg_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{y})$, so $\E[e(H_{k, i})] = |\mathbf{X}|^2 \cdot O(1/\sqrt{n}) = O(\sqrt{n})$. By Markov's inequality, for large enough $n$,
    \[
      \prob\bigl[e(H_{k, i}) = O(\sqrt{n})\bigr] \geq 1 - \frac{\E[e(H_{k, i})]}{\omega(\sqrt{n})} \geq 1 - \frac{\beta}{400M},
    \]
    in which case Tur\'{a}n's theorem yields an independent set $\mathbf{Y}_{k, i}$ of size $n/(2O(\sqrt{n}) + \sqrt{n}) = 2\gamma \sqrt{n}$, for some constant $\gamma = \gamma(\beta, M) > 0$. In particular, $\deg_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x})$ is distinct for $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{Y}_{k, i}$. The expected proportion of values of $i$ for which this fails to occur is $\beta/400M$, and by Markov's inequality again, it fails only for $\beta/2M$ proportion of values of $i$ with probability at least $1 - (\beta/400M)/(\beta/2M) = 0.995$, in which case put $\mathcal{I}_k$ as the set of $i$ that succeeds.

    By Property~2 of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}, $\E[\deg_U(\mathbf{x})] = d/2 + O(\sqrt{n})$ for each $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$. Since $\deg_U(\mathbf{x})$ follows a binomial distribution with parameters $O(n)$ and $1/2$, we have $\Var(\deg_U(\mathbf{x})) = O(n)$. By triangle inequality, 
    \[
      |\deg_U(\mathbf{x}) - d/2| \leq \bigl|\deg_U(\mathbf{x}) - \E[\deg_U(\mathbf{x})]\bigr| + O(\sqrt{n}),
    \]
    so for sufficiently large $Q$, Chebyshev's gives
    \[
      \prob\left[|\deg_U(\mathbf{x}) - d/2| > Q\sqrt{n}\right] \leq \frac{\Var\bigl(\deg_U(\mathbf{x})\bigr)}{Q^2 n} = \frac{O(1)}{Q^2} < \frac{\gamma}{200}.
    \]
    Let $\mathbf{Y}$ be the set of all $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$ such that $|\deg_U(\mathbf{x}) - d/2| \leq Q\sqrt{n}$. Then each $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$ is not present in $\mathbf{Y}$ with probability $< \gamma/200$. By Markov's,
    \[
      \prob\bigl[|\mathbf{Y}| \geq (1 - \gamma)|\mathbf{X}|\bigr] = 1 - \prob\bigl[|\mathbf{X} \setminus \mathbf{Y}| \geq \gamma|\mathbf{X}|\bigr] \geq 1 - \frac{\gamma|\mathbf{X}|}{200\gamma |\mathbf{X}|} = 0.995.
    \]
    Hence, with probability $\geq 0.995$, for sufficiently large $Q$ we obtain $\mathbf{Y} \subseteq \mathbf{X}$ of size $\geq (1 - \gamma)|\mathbf{X}|$ whose elements lie in the interval $[d/2 - Q\sqrt{n},\; d/2 + Q\sqrt{n}]$.

    By the union bound, both of the above events occur with probability $\geq 0.99$, in which case take $\mathbf{X}_{k, i} = \mathbf{Y}_{k, i} \cap \mathbf{Y}$ for $i \in \mathcal{I}_k$. Since $|\mathbf{Y}_{k, i}| \geq 2\gamma \sqrt{n}$ and $|\mathbf{Y}| \geq (1 - \gamma)|\mathbf{X}|$, we have $|\mathbf{X}_{k, i}| \geq \gamma \sqrt{n}$. This proves Property~1.

    \item[2.] Since
    \[
      e_{k, i} = e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) + e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) = e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) + \sum_{u \in U_0} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} \cdot \deg_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u),
    \]
    $e_{k, i}$ is of $(O(n), 1/2)$-Littlewood--Offord type with coefficients $\deg_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u) = O(\sqrt{n})$. Thus $\Var(e_{k, 0}) = O(n^2)$ and by Chebyshev's,
    \[
      \prob\bigl[|e_{k, 0} - \E[e_{k, 0}]| \leq Qn\bigr] \geq 1 - \frac{\Var(e_{k, 0})}{Q^2 n^2} = 0.99,
    \]
    for large enough $Q$. We end this proof by noting that enlarging $Q$ does not fail Property~1.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of the main lemma}

We assemble Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} and Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure} to prove the main lemma. 

Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} to obtain $U_0, \mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}, c', d$, and define $\mathcal{P}, U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}, U_{k, i}$ as in Section~\ref{sec:double-exposure}. Note that $U$ is the only source of randomness. We prove the main lemma with $W$ being the set of all vertices in $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$.

Recall that $k$ ranges over the interval between $c'\sqrt{n}$ and $2c'\sqrt{n}$. For each $k$, the union bound shows that all four properties of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure} hold with probability $\geq 0.4$. Let $\mathcal{K}$ be the set of all such $k$. Then $\E\bigl[c'\sqrt{n} - |\mathcal{K}|\bigr] \leq 0.6 \, c'\sqrt{n}$, and Markov's inequality yields
\[
  \prob\left[|\mathcal{K}| \geq 0.2 \, c'\sqrt{n}\right] = 1 - \prob\left[c'\sqrt{n} - |\mathcal{K}| \geq 0.8 \, c'\sqrt{n}\right] \geq 1 - \frac{\E\bigl[c'\sqrt{n} - |\mathcal{K}|\bigr]}{0.8 \, c'\sqrt{n}} \geq 0.25.
\]
We now show that $e(U)$ is close to $m$ with high probability. Note that $|m - \E[e(U)]| = O(n^{3/2})$, as $\E[e(U)] = e(U_0)/4$ and $|e(U_0) - 4m| = O(n^{3/2})$ from Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}. Recall that $\Var(e(U)) = O(n^3)$. Since $|e(U) - m| \leq |e(U) - \E[e(U)]| + |\E[e(U)] - m|$, Chebyshev's inequality yields
\[
  \prob\left[|e(U) - m| = O(n^{3/2})\right] \geq \prob\left[|e(U) - \E[e(U)]| = O(n^{3/2})\right] \geq 1 - \frac{\Var(e(U))}{\omega(n^{3})} \geq 0.9,
\]
for large enough $n$. Fix an outcome $U$ satisfying both events above, which hold together with probability $\geq 0.25 + 0.9 - 1 = 0.15$. Let $Q, \beta, M, \mathcal{I}_k$ be given by Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure}. 

For each $k$, 
\[
  \E[e_{k, 0}] - \E[e_{k - 1, 0}] = \frac{1}{2}\bigl[e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, 0}, U_0) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k - 1, 0}, U_0)\bigr] + O(\sqrt{n}) = \Theta(n),
\]
as $\E[\deg_U(\mathbf{x})] = \deg_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})/2 = \Theta(n)$ for $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{S}$. We find a subset of $\mathcal{K}$ whose realized values $e_{k, 0}$ are well-separated by $\Theta(n)$. Specifically, let $\mathcal{K}' \subseteq \mathcal{K}$ contain every $q$th element of $\mathcal{K}$, for sufficiently large $q$ such that the values of $\E[e_{k, 0}]$ are separated by $\geq 4Qn$ for $k \in \mathcal{K}'$. Then, $|\mathcal{K}'| = \Theta(\sqrt{n})$ and Property~2 of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure} ensures the values of $e_{k, 0}$ are separated by $\geq 2Qn$ for $k \in \mathcal{K}'$. 

We now partition the linear gaps between the values of $e_{k, 0} : k \in \mathcal{K}'$ into square-root sized gaps. Let $k \in \mathcal{K}'$. By Property~3 of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure}, $e_{k, c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k, 0} \geq 3\beta n$. Consider the range of integers between $e_{k, 0}$ and $e_{k, 0} + 3\beta n$. Within this range, we take $2\beta\sqrt{n}/M$ intervals of length $M\sqrt{n}$, each separated by a distance of $M\sqrt{n}/2 = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$. By Property~4 of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure}, $\leq \beta \sqrt{n} / M$ of these intervals contain no value of $e_{k, i}$. Consider the $\beta\sqrt{n}/M$ intervals that do contain a value of $e_{k, i}$ and pick a representative $e_{k, i}$ from each. Let $\mathcal{I}'_k$ be the set of indices $i$ corresponding to these representatives, and let $\mathcal{I}_k'' = \mathcal{I}_k \cap \mathcal{I}'_k$. Then 
\[
  |\mathcal{I}_k''| = |\mathcal{I}_k| + |\mathcal{I}'_k| - |\mathcal{I}_k \cup \mathcal{I}'_k| \geq \beta\sqrt{n}/M - \beta c' \sqrt{n}/2M = \Omega(\sqrt{n})
\]
and each $e_{k, i}$ is separated by $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ for $i \in \mathcal{I}_k''$. Recall that $Q \geq 3\beta$, so by construction $|e_{k, i} - e_{k, 0}| \leq 3\beta n \leq Qn$ for $i \in \mathcal{I}_k''$. In particular, amongst choices of $k \in \mathcal{K}'$ and $\mathcal{I}_k''$, we already have $\Omega(n)$ distinct values of $e(U_{k, i})$ that are separated by $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$. It remains to fill the square-root sized gaps between these values. 

In increasing order of $e(U_{k, i})$, let $\mathcal{P}' \subseteq \bigl\{(k, i) : k \in \mathcal{K}', i \in \mathcal{I}_k''\bigr\}$ be the corresponding index set of every $q$th of these values. We enlarge $q$ so that $e(U_{k, i})$ from $\mathcal{P}'$ are separated by $\geq 2Q\sqrt{n}$. For each $(k, i) \in \mathcal{P}'$ we have $i \in \mathcal{I}_k$, so Property~1 of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure} furnishes a set $\mathbf{X}_{k, i}$ such that each $\deg_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x})$ is unique for $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}_{k, i}$ while lying in a fixed interval of length $2Q\sqrt{n}$ centred at $d/2$, where $d$ is independent of $k$ and $i$. Thus for each $(k, i) \in \mathcal{P}'$, the set
\[
  \bigl\{e(U_{k, i} \sqcup \mathbf{x}_{k, i}) : \mathbf{x}_{k, i} \in \mathbf{X}_{k, i}\bigr\}
\] 
has size $|\mathbf{X}_{k, i}| = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$ and lies in an interval of length $2Q\sqrt{n}$ centred at $e(U_{k, i}) + d/2$, so these sets do not overlap among different $(k, i) \in \mathcal{P}'$. Therefore, 
\[
  \left|\bigl\{e(U \sqcup Z) : Z \subseteq W\bigr\}\right| \geq \sum_{(k, i) \in \mathcal{P}'}\left|\bigl\{e(U_{k, i} \sqcup \mathbf{x}_{k, i}) : \mathbf{x}_{k, i} \in \mathbf{X}_{k, i}\bigr\}\right| = \Omega(n^{3/2}). 
\]
\qed
