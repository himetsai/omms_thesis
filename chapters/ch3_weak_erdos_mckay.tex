\section{Subgraphs of Quadratically Many Sizes}
\label{sec:ch3}

Recall that Erdős and Mckay conjectured that for any Ramsey graph $G$ on $n$ vertices, there exists $\delta = \delta(C) > 0$ such that
\[
  \{1, 2, \ldots, \delta n^2\} \subseteq \Phi(G) \coloneqq \{e(H) : H \text{ is an induced subgraph of } G\},
\] 
That is, for any $1 \leq t \leq \delta n^2$, there exists an induced subgraph with exactly $t$ edges. 
% In \cite{erdosmckayrandomgraph}, the conjecture was proven for random graphs, and so it is natural to ask whether Ramsey graphs also exhibit this property. 
The conjecture was widely open for decades. In 2003, Alon, Krivelevich and Sudakov \cite{AlonKrivelevichSudakov2003} proved the conjecture with $n^{\delta}$ in place of $\delta n^2$, but little progress was made afterwards. 
\begin{theorem}[Alon, Krivelevich and Sudakov {\cite{AlonKrivelevichSudakov2003}}]
  \label{thm:alks2003}
  For any $C > 0$, there exists $\delta = \delta(C) > 0$ such that
  \[
  \{1, 2, \ldots, n^{\delta}\} \subseteq \Phi(G).
  \]
\end{theorem}
Since the conjecture implies $|\Phi(G)| = \Omega(n^2)$ and the triavial upper bound gives $|\Phi(G)| \leq \binom{n}{2}$, a natural relaxation of the conjecture is to ask whether $\Phi(G)$ indeed has quadratic size asymptotically. In 2017, Narayanan, Sahasrabudhe, and Tomon \cite{narayanan2017ramseygraphsinducesubgraphs} proved a near optimal bound of $|\Phi(G)| = n^{2 - o(1)}$ by building on the notion of diversity from Chapter~\ref{sec:ch2}. The desired quadratic bound was achieved by Kwan and Sudakov \cite{kwan2021ramseygraphsinducesubgraphs} a few years later, and this chapter is dedicated to presenting their proof.

\begin{theorem}[Kwan and Sudakov {\cite{kwan2021ramseygraphsinducesubgraphs}}]
  \label{thm:ks}
  For any $C > 0$, 
  \[
    |\Phi(G)| = \Theta(n^2).
  \]
\end{theorem}

Recall that Theorem \ref{thm:bs2007} provides a bound of $|\Phi(G)| = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$ for any Ramsey graph $G$ by removing a single vertex to construct each subgraph. Two ideas may be used to improve this construction. First, we can apply the same idea to many different induced subgraphs rather than merely $G$ itself. Second, instead of deleting only one vertex, we may add and remove various combinations of vertices to produce many more distinct sizes. 

Specifically, we pick a target $m$ and find two disjoint vertex sets $U$ and $W$, where $U$ is a linear-sized random set with $e(U) \approx m$ and $W$ is an augmenting set of size $O(\sqrt{n})$ selected from a candidate set $W_0 \subseteq V(G) \setminus U$ whose degrees are of linear order and all lie in a narrow interval. We then show that the subsets $Z \subseteq W$ produce $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ distinct values of $e(U \cup Z)$ that all lie in an interval of length $O(n^{3/2})$ centred at $e(U)$. Applying this to $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ well-separated targets $m$ yields the full $\Omega(n^2)$ count.

Kwan and Sudakov construct $W$ as three disjoint parts $\mathbf{S}$, $\mathbf{T}$, $\mathbf{X}$, each of size $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$, that act at complementary scales. Every element has $\Theta(n)$ edges into $U$, and the degrees from $\mathbf{S}$ into $U$ are uniformly smaller than those from $\mathbf{T}$ by a gap of $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$. Varying the number $k$ of elements of $\mathbf{S}$ included in $Z$ shifts $e(U \cup Z)$ at the coarsest scale $\Theta(n)$. Exchanging elements of $\mathbf{S}$ for elements of $\mathbf{T}$ shifts it by $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$. The elements of $\mathbf{X}$ fill the remaining $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ gap by contributing $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ distinct degrees into $U \cup Z$ for each choice of $Z$. Hence the different combinations of $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$ yield $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ distinct values of $e(U \cup Z)$.

Achieving the last requirement that $\mathbf{X}$ provides $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ distinct degrees into $U \cup Z$ for each choice of $Z$ is the main technical challenge, as vertex diversity defined in Chapter~\ref{sec:ch2} does not produce enough distinct degrees in this case. In particular, by the anticoncentration principle from Section~\ref{sec:anticoncentration}, we have $\prob[d_U(x) = d_U(y)] = O(1/\sqrt{n})$ for all but at most $n^{\delta}$ pairs $x, y \in V(G)$ in an $(\eps, \delta)$-diverse graph. Following the same construction as in the proof of Lemma \ref{lemma:diversity-degrees}, the collision graph $H$ on the candidate set $W_0$ has expected number of edges
\[
  \E[e(H)] = O(|W_0|^2/\sqrt{n} + |W_0| \cdot n^{\delta}),
\]
which yields an independent set $I \subseteq W_0$ of size 
\[
  |I| = \frac{|W_0|^2}{2\E[e(H)] + |W_0|} = \Omega\left(\sqrt{n} \cdot \frac{|W_0|}{|W_0| + n^{1/2 + \delta}}\right).
\]
by Tur\'{a}n's theorem (Proposition~\ref{prop:turan}). Thus for $|I|$ to reach $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$, we need $|W_0| = \Omega(n^{1/2 + \delta})$. Unfortunately, $W_0$ is extracted by pigeonholing $n$ vertices into $O(\sqrt{n})$ degree intervals of length $O(\sqrt{n})$, so at best we have $|W_0| = \Theta(\sqrt{n})$, yielding only $|I| = \Omega(n^{1/2 - \delta})$.

To overcome this obstacle, Kwan and Sudakov's relaxes the canditate set $W_0$ to also contain disjoint \textit{pairs}. Write $\mathbf{x}$ in bold denote an object that is either a single vertex or a pair of vertices, and similarly for sets of such objects. For $\mathbf{x} = \{x_1, x_2\}$, set $N(\mathbf{x}) \coloneqq N(x_1) \cup N(x_2)$ as a multiset union and $d(\mathbf{x}) \coloneqq |N(\mathbf{x})| = d(x_1) + d(x_2)$ as the pair-degree, and note that there are $\binom{n}{2} = \Theta(n^2)$ pairs but the pair-degrees take only $O(n)$ values. Pigeonhole now yields
$\Theta(n)$ disjoint pairs sharing a common pair-degree, vastly exceeding the $n^{1/2 + \delta}$ threshold. The price is that we need the symmetric difference $|N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})|$ to be $\Omega(n)$ not just for most pairs of vertices, but for most pairs of \emph{pairs}. We call this property \textit{pair-diversity}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:pair_diverse}
  An $n$-vertex graph $G$ is \textit{$(\eps, \delta, \alpha)_2$-diverse} if for each pair of vertices $\mathbf{x} = \{x_1, x_2\}$ in $G$ with $|N(x_1) \Delta N(x_2)| \geq \alpha n$, there are at most $n^{\delta}$ disjoint pairs of vertices $\mathbf{y} = \{y_1, y_2\}$ with $\mathbf{x} \cap \mathbf{y} = \emptyset$, such that $|N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})| < \eps n$.
\end{definition}

It turns out that richness not only implies diversity, but also pair-diversity.

\begin{lemma}
  Let an $n$-vertex graph $G$ be $(\eps, \delta)$-rich, with $\delta \leq 1/2$. Then $G$ is $(\eps, \delta, \alpha)_2$-diverse for any $\alpha \geq 2\delta$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  Suppose not. Then there exists a pair $\mathbf{x} = \{x_1, x_2\}$ with $|N(x_1) \Delta \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq \alpha n$ and a set $Y$ of $n^{\delta}$ pairs such that $|N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})| < \alpha \eps n /2$ for all $\mathbf{y} \in Y$. Since
  \[
    |N(x_1) \Delta \overline{N(x_2)}| = |N(x_1) \cap N(x_2)|  + |\overline{N(x_1)} \cap \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq \alpha n,
  \]
  we have $|N(x_1) \cap N(x_2)| \geq (\alpha/2)n \geq \delta n$ or $|\overline{N(x_1)} \cap \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq (\alpha/2)n \geq \delta n$. Assume without loss of generality that the former case holds. Then for each vertex $y$ in any $\mathbf{y} \in Y$, 
  \[
    \left|\overline{N(y)} \cap (N(x_1) \cap N(x_2))\right| \leq |N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})| < \alpha \eps n /2 \leq \eps |N(x_1) \cap N(x_2)|.
  \]
  But then the set of all such $y$ now contradicts the $(\eps, \delta)$-rich condition.
\end{proof}

We also introduce a reversed notion of diversity.

\begin{lemma}
  Let an $n$-vertex graph $G$ be $(\eps, \delta)$-rich. Then $G$ has at most $n^{1 + \delta}$ pairs of vertices $x_1, x_2 \in V(G)$ with $|N(x_1) \Delta \overline{N(x_2)}| < (\eps/2)n$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  We show that for each choice of $x_1 \in V(G)$, there are at most $n^{\delta}$ vertices $x_2 \in V(G)$ with $|N(x_1) \Delta \overline{N(x_2)}| < (\eps/2)n$. Since either $|N(x_1)| \geq n/2$ or $|\overline{N(x_1)}| \geq n/2$, we assume without loss of generality that $|N(x_1)| \geq n/2$. By $(\eps, \delta)$-richness, there are at most $n^{\delta}$ vertices $x_2$ with $|N(x_2) \cap N(x_1)| < \eps n/2$. For all other choices of $x_2$, 
  \[
    |N(x_1) \Delta \overline{N(x_2)}| \geq |N(x_1) \cap N(x_2)|  \geq (\eps/2) n.
  \]
  The result now follows from the fact that there are only $n$ choices of $x_1$.
\end{proof}

\textit{Remark:} In contrast to diversity, this lemma gives an upper bound on the symmetric difference between the neighbourhoods of two vertices. Specifically, since $|N(x_1) \Delta N(x_2)| + |N(x_1) \Delta \overline{N(x_2)}| = n - 2$ for any two vertices $x_1, x_2$,
\[
  |N(x_1) \Delta N(x_2)| \leq (1 - \eps/2)n
\]  
for most pairs of vertices $x_1, x_2$. 

One last thing we need is to ensure $\mathbf{X}$ actually provides $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ distinct degrees into $U \cup \mathbf{Z} \coloneqq U \cup \bigcup_{\mathbf{z} \in \mathbf{Z}} \mathbf{z}$ for each choice of augmenting set $\mathbf{Z} \subseteq \mathbf{S} \cup \mathbf{T}$. Notice that the current construction of $\mathbf{X}$ only ensures distinct degrees $d_U(\mathbf{x})$ into $U$ instead of $d_{U \cup Z}(\mathbf{x})$. If $U$ were sampled once and directly constructed $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$ based on $U$, then the $d_U(\mathbf{x})$ would be fixed numbers given the outcome of $U$. However, there is no guarantee that $d_{U \cup Z}(\mathbf{x}) = d_{U}(\mathbf{x}) + d_{Z}(\mathbf{x})$ would remain distinct, as the addition of $d_{Z}(\mathbf{x})$ could cancel out the uniqueness of $d_{U}(\mathbf{x})$. Kwan and Sudakov overcome this with a \textit{double-exposure} technique. They perform two rounds of sampling. In the first round, they sampled the random set $U_0$ and construct $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$ relative to $U_0$. In the second round, they sampled $U$ from $U_0$ by independently keeping each vertex with probability $1/2$. After the first sampling, the construction guarantees $|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$ for all $x, y \in \mathbf{X}$, and the augmenting sets $\mathbf{Z} \subseteq \mathbf{S} \cup \mathbf{T}$ are fully determined by $U_0$. In the second round of sampling, the difference $d_U(\mathbf{x}) - d_U(\mathbf{y})$ becomes a sum of $\Omega(n)$ independent Bernoulli terms, and Littlewood-Offord yields $\prob[d_U(\mathbf{x}) - d_U(\mathbf{y}) = t] = O(1/\sqrt{n})$ for every integer $t$. In particular, since $t_{xy} = d_Z(\mathbf{y}) - d_Z(\mathbf{x})$ is a fixed integer for each $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$, we may bound for the collision probability
\[
  \prob[d_{U \cup Z}(\mathbf{x}) = d_{U \cup Z}(\mathbf{y})] = \prob[d_{U}(\mathbf{x}) - d_{U}(\mathbf{y}) = t_{xy}] = O(1/\sqrt{n}).
\]
The same collision graph construction now finds us the desired set of $U$ such that $d_{U \cup Z}(\mathbf{x})$ is distinct for $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$.

\subsection{The main lemma}

As outlined above, we will find many induced subgraphs $G[U]$ whose edge counts are well-separated from each other, and show that each can be augmented in $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ ways. Theorem \ref{thm:ks} will be an immediate consequence of the following lemma, which we will dedicate the rest of this chapter to prove.

\begin{lemma}
  \label{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts}

  Let $C > 0$. Then there exists $c = c(C) > 0$, such that for any $C$-Ramsey graph $G$ on $n$ vertices and any $m$ satisfying $cn^2 \leq m \leq 2cn^2$, there are disjoint subsets $U, W \subseteq V(G)$ with $|e(U) - m| = O(n^{3/2})$ and $|W| = O(\sqrt{n})$ such that 
  \[
    |\{e(U \cup Z) : Z \subseteq W\}| = \Omega(n^{3/2}).
  \]
\end{lemma}

We first show how this implies Theorem \ref{thm:ks}.

\paragraph{Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:ks}.} Note that for any $U, W$ given by Lemma \ref{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts},  
\[
  |e(U \cup W) - e(U)| = O(n^{3/2}),
\]
as $|W| = O(\sqrt{n})$ and each $w \in W$ has at most $n$ neighbours in $U \cup W$. But then the $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ values of $e(U \cup Z)$ for $Z \subseteq W$ are contained in an interval of length $O(n^{3/2})$ centered at $e(U)$. Partition the interval between $cn^2$ and $2cn^2$ into $\frac{c}{k}\sqrt{n}$ subintervals of length $kn^{3/2}$, for some large enough constant $k$ to be determined. Now apply Lemma \ref{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts} to a suitable choice of $m$ in each subinterval so that the obtained values of $e(U \cup Z)$ for $Z \subseteq W$ lie in the same subinterval as $m$, which is allowed for sufficiently large $k$. Since we applied Lemma \ref{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts} to $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ disjoint subintervals, we obtain $\Omega(n^2)$ distinct sizes of induced subgraphs. \qed

\subsection{The construction of augmenting sets}
\label{sec:construction}

We now prove the key construction that produces the sets $U_0, \mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$
with the properties needed for Lemma~\ref{lemma:well-separated-edge-counts}.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:construction}
For any $C > 0$ there exists $c = c(C) > 0$ such that the following holds. For any $C$-Ramsey graph $G$ on $n$ vertices and any $m$ satisfying $cn^2 \le m \le 2cn^2$, there is a vertex set $U_0 \subseteq V(G)$ with $|e(U_0) - 4m| = O(n^{3/2})$, a constant $c' = c'(C) > 0$, and sets $\mathbf{S}$, $\mathbf{T}$, $\mathbf{X}$ of size $c'\sqrt{n}$ such that
\begin{enumerate}
    \item either $U_0, \mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X} \subseteq V(G)$ are disjoint sets of
        vertices, or $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X} \subseteq \binom{V(G)}{2}$ are sets of
        disjoint pairs of vertices with no vertex appearing in more
        than one of $U_0, \mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$,
    \item there is $d = \Theta(n)$ such that
        $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = d + O(\sqrt{n})$ for each
        $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{S} \cup \mathbf{T} \cup \mathbf{X}$,
    \item $\min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{T}} d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) -
        \max_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{S}} d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \geq 8c'\sqrt{n}$,
    \item for each $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{X}}{2}$, we
        have $|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})|
        = \Omega(n)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

Property~2 ensures that each element contributes $\Theta(n)$ edges to any subgraph containing $U_0$, so that adding or removing a single element shifts the edge count by $\Theta(n)$. Property~3 guarantees that swapping an element of $\mathbf{S}$ for an element of $\mathbf{T}$ shifts the edge count by $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$. Property~4 is the anticoncentration condition on $\mathbf{X}$ that sets up the double-exposure step. 

The proof will start with reducing to a rich subgraph of linear size using Lemma~\ref{lem:rich-subgraph} and using the pigeonhole principle to obtain a large candidate set $\mathbf{W}_0$ of vertices or pairs with similar degrees, satisfying Property~1 and Property~2. We will then sample $U_0$ at random and verify that all desired properties hold simultaneously with positive probability.

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}]

Let $\varepsilon = \varepsilon(C)$ be given by Lemma~\ref{lem:rich-subgraph} and set $\delta = \varepsilon/4$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:rich-subgraph}, $G$ contains an $(\varepsilon, \delta)$-rich induced subgraph $G[V']$ on $\Omega(n)$ vertices. Since $\log |V'| \ge \frac{1}{2}\log n$, the graph $G[V']$ is $2C$-Ramsey, so by adjusting constants it suffices to work inside $G[V']$. In particular, we may assume $G$ itself is $(\varepsilon, \delta)$-rich.

By Lemma~\ref{lem:richness-implies} with $\alpha = \varepsilon/2$,
this richness assumption furnishes the following three properties:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(a)] $G$ is $(\varepsilon/2, \delta)$-diverse,
  \item[(b)] $G$ is $(\varepsilon^2/4, \delta, \varepsilon/2)_2$-diverse,
  \item[(c)] there are at most $n^{1+\delta}$ pairs
      $\{x_1, x_2\}$ with
      $|N(x_1) \Delta \overline{N(x_2)}| < (\varepsilon/2)n$.
\end{enumerate}

Each of the $\binom{n}{2} = \Theta(n^2)$ vertex pairs $\{x_1, x_2\}$ has a pair-degree $d(\{x_1, x_2\}) = d(x_1) + d(x_2)$ taking values from $0$ to $2n$, and so by the pigeonhole principle there exists a value $d^*$ and a collection $H$ of $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ pairs with $d(\mathbf{x}) = d^* + O(\sqrt{n})$ for all $\mathbf{x} \in H$. By the remark after Lemma \ref{lem:richness-implies}, $d^* = \Theta(n)$. Interpret $H$ as a graph on $V(G)$ whose edges are the $\Omega(n^{3/2})$ pairs. By (c), we may delete $O(n^{1+\delta}) = o(n^{3/2})$ edges in $H$ so that all remaining pairs have $|N(x_1) \Delta \overline{N(x_2)}| \ge (\varepsilon/2)n$. Suppose there exists vertex $v$ in $H$ with degree $\Omega(n^{3/4})$. Take $\mathbf{W}_0 = N_H(v)$ and set $d = d^* - d(v)$. Then each $w \in \mathbf{W}_0$ is a single vertex with degree $d(w) = d + O(\sqrt{n})$. Now suppose $H$ has maximum degree $\Delta = o(n^{3/4})$. By iteratively picking an edge and removing at most $2\Delta$ edges adjacent to it, we obtain a matching of size $\Omega(n^{3/2})/o(n^{3/4}) = \Omega(n^{3/4})$. Take $\mathbf{W}_0$ to be the pairs in this matching and set $d = d^*$. In either case, the subsets of $\mathbf{W}_0$ satisfy Property~1 and Property~2.

Next, we extract a subset $\mathbf{A} \subseteq \mathbf{W}_0$ of size $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$
with pairwise large symmetric differences. Define graph $F$ on $\mathbf{L}$ by joining $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$ whenever $|N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})| < (\varepsilon^2/4)n$. By diversity, the graph $F$ has $|\mathbf{W}_0| = \Omega(n^{3/4})$ vertices and maximum degree at most $n^{\delta} < n^{1/4}$. Thus by Turán's theorem, $F$ has an independent set $\mathbf{A} \subseteq \mathbf{W}_0$ with size $\Omega(|\mathbf{W}_0|/n^{1/4}) = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$. That is, for $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$, we have $|N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$.

By Corollary~1.4 and the Ramsey property, $e(G) \ge 800cn^2$ for some constant $c = c(C) > 0$. For $cn^2 \le m \le 2cn^2$, set $p = \sqrt{4m/e(G)}$, so that $p = \Omega(1)$ and $p \le 0.1$. Let $U_0$ be a random subset of $V(G)$ formed by including each vertex independently with probability $p$. We make the following claim:

\begin{claim}
  The following five events each hold with probability greater than $4/5$, and therefore all hold simultaneously with positive probability by the union bound.

  \begin{enumerate}
    \item[(i)] $|e(U_0) - 4m| = O(n^{3/2})$,
    \item[(ii)] There is a set $\mathbf{Q} \subseteq \mathbf{A}$ involving no vertices of $U_0$, with $|\mathbf{Q}| \ge (2/3)|\mathbf{A}|$,
    \item[(iii)] There is a set $\mathbf{R} \subseteq \mathbf{A}$ with $|\mathbf{R}| \ge (2/3)|\mathbf{A}|$ and $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = pd + O(\sqrt{n})$ for each $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{R}$,
    \item[(iv)] $|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$ for each $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$,
    \item[(v)] The equality $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = d_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})$ holds for $O(\sqrt{n})$ pairs $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{claim}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Claim]
  We prove each part in order.
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item[(i)] Note that $\E[e(U_0)] = 4m$ and there are $O(n^3)$ pairs of edges in $G$ whose presense are dependent in $G[U_0]$, as this only occur if two edges share a vertex. For $e \in E(G)$, let $A_e$ be the event that $e$ is present in $G[U_0]$. Since
    \[
      \Var(e(U_0)) \leq \E[e(U_0)] + \sum_{e \sim e'} \prob(A_e \cap A_{e'}) = O(n^3),
    \]
    it follows from Chebyshev's inequality that
    \[
      \prob[|e(U_0) - 4m| = \omega(n^{3/2})] \leq \frac{O(n^3)}{\omega(n^{3})} \to 0,
    \]
    as $n \to \infty$.
    \item[(ii)] Let $\mathbf{Q}$ be the subset of $\mathbf{A}$ involving no vertices of $U_0$. Then $|\mathbf{Q}|$ follows a binomial distribution with $|\mathbf{A}|$ trials, it has mean at least $(1 - p)^2|\mathbf{A}| \geq 0.8|\mathbf{A}|$ and variance $O(|\mathbf{A}|)$. It follows from Chebyshev's inequality that
    \[
      \prob\left[|\mathbf{Q}| < \frac{2}{3}|\mathbf{A}|\right] \leq \prob\left[|\mathbf{Q}| - \E[|\mathbf{Q}|] \geq 0.1|\mathbf{A}|\right] \leq \frac{O(|\mathbf{A}|)}{0.01|\mathbf{A}|^2} = O(1/|\mathbf{A}|) \to 0,
    \]
    as $n \to \infty$. 
    \item[(iii)] For $\mathbf{x} \in |\mathbf{A}|$, the degree $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})$ has mean $pd + O(\sqrt{n})$ and variance $O(n)$. By Chebyshev's inequality, 
    \[
      \prob[d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = pd + O(\sqrt{n})] \leq 1 - \prob[|d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) - \E[d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})]| = \omega(\sqrt{n})] \leq 1 - \frac{O(n)}{\omega(n)} \to 1.
    \]
    In particular, $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = pd + O(\sqrt{n})$ with probability $0.99$ when $n$ is sufficiently large. Let $\mathbf{R} \subseteq \mathbf{A}$ be the set of $\mathbf{x}$ that satisfies this bound. Since $\E[|\mathbf{A} \setminus \mathbf{R}|] = 0.01|\mathbf{A}| $. It now follows from Markov's inequality that 
    \[
      \prob\left[|\mathbf{R}| \geq \frac{2}{3}|\mathbf{A}|\right] = 1 - \prob\left[|\mathbf{A} \setminus \mathbf{R}| \geq \frac{1}{3}|\mathbf{A}|\right] \geq 1 - \frac{\E[|\mathbf{A} \setminus \mathbf{R}|]}{\frac{1}{3}|\mathbf{A}|} \geq 0.8.
    \]
    \item[(iv)] Recall that $|N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$ for $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$. Since $|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| = |N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y}) \cap U_0|$, it has a binomial distribution with parameters $N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})$ and $p$. By the Chernoff bound, 
    \[
      \prob\left[|N_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})| < \frac{p}{2}|N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})|\right] = e^{-\Omega(n)} = o(n)
    \]
    The result now follows from the union bound.
    \item[(v)] For $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$, note that the random variable $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) - d_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})$ is of $(|N(\mathbf(x)) \Delta N(\mathbf(y)), p|)$-Littlewood-Offord type. Since $|N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$, we have $\prob[d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = d_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})] = O(1/\sqrt{n})$, and so the expected number of paris $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{A}}{2}$ satisfying $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = d_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})$ is thus $O(\sqrt{n})$. The result now follows from Markov's inequality.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

Fix an outcome of $U_0$ for which all five events hold. Let $\mathbf{A}' = \mathbf{Q} \cap \mathbf{R}$, and note that $|\mathbf{A}'| \geq |\mathbf{A}|/3 = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$. It remains to obtain $\mathbf{S}$, $\mathbf{T}$, and $\mathbf{X}$ from $\mathbf{A}'$ that satisfy Properties~2--4. Consider the graph on $\mathbf{A}'$ where $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$ are adjacent if and only if $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) = d_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})$. By Turán Theorem (Proposition~\ref{prop:turan}), there is an independent set $\mathbf{W} \subseteq \mathbf{A}'$ of size $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$, and note that $\mathbf{W}$ has distinct degrees into $U_0$. Partition $\mathbf{W}$ into three equal parts by the degree $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})$ into $U_0$, and let $\mathbf{T}$ be the top third, $\mathbf{S}$ be the bottom third, and $\mathbf{X}$ be the middle third. Since $\mathbf{W}$ has distinct degrees and $|\mathbf{X}| = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$, we may find constant $c'$ that satisfies Property~3. Since $\mathbf{X} \subseteq \mathbf{A}$, Property~4 holds. Trimming the sizes of $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$ to $c'\sqrt{n}$ completes the proof.
\end{proof}


\textit{Remark:} Claim~\ref{claim:few-degree-collisions} is where the
anticoncentration principle from Section~1.2 enters the proof. For
each pair $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbf{A}$ with
$|N(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N(\mathbf{y})| = \Omega(n)$, the
difference $d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x}) - d_{U_0}(\mathbf{y})$ is a sum of
$\Omega(n)$ independent bounded random variables (since $U_0$ is
formed by independent Bernoulli sampling). By the
Erd\H{o}s--Littlewood--Offord theorem, the probability that this sum
equals zero is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$. This is the Bernoulli-sampling case
of the anticoncentration mechanism discussed in Section~1.2, in
contrast to the hypergeometric case used in Lemma~2.2.

\subsection{The double-exposure argument}
\label{sec:double-exposure}

Given $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, c'$ from Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}, fix an ordering on $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{T}$ and define
\[
  \mathcal{P} \coloneqq \{(k, i) : c'\sqrt{n} \leq k \leq 2c'\sqrt{n} \text{ and } 0 \leq i \leq c'\sqrt{n}\}.
\]
For $(k, i) \in \mathcal{P}$, define $\mathbf{Z}_{k, i} \subseteq \mathbf{S} \sqcup \mathbf{T}$ by taking the first $k - i$ elements from $\mathbf{S}$ and the first $i$ elements from $\mathbf{T}$. By Property 2 and 3 of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}, the values of $e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) \coloneqq \sum_{\mathbf{z} \in \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}} d_{U_0}(\mathbf{z})$ are evenly spaced by $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ as $(k, i)$ vary lexicographically, which already forms $\Omega(n)$ distinct values of $e(U_0 \cup \mathbf{Z}_{k, i})$. What remains is to fill the $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ gaps between each $e(U_0 \cup \mathbf{Z}_{k, i})$ with $\mathbf{X}$ via the \textit{double-exposure} technique while maintaining the nice gap struture of the subsets $\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}$. More specifically, we sample $U$ from $U_0$ such that the values $e(U \cup \mathbf{Z}_{k, i} \cup \{\mathbf{x}\})$ are distinct as $\mathbf{Z}_{k, i} \subseteq \mathbf{S} \sqcup \mathbf{T}$ and $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$ vary. Define $U_{k,i} \coloneqq U \cup \mathbf{Z}_{k,i}$ and $e_{k, i} \coloneqq e(U_{k, i}) - e(U) = e(\mathbf{Z}_{k,0}) + e(\mathbf{Z}_{k,0}, U)$. There are four things we need to ensure: 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Degree distinctness in $\mathbf{X}$.} For most pairs $(k, i)$, the elements of $\mathbf{X}$ should have $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ pairwise distinct degrees into $U_{k,i}$, all lying in an interval of length $O(\sqrt{n})$. This is the payoff of double-exposure.
  \item \emph{Concentration of the edge count.} The value $e_{k,0}$ should concentrate around its expectation within $O(n)$, so that the $\Theta(n)$ spacing between $e_{k, 0}$ and $e_{k + 1, 0}$ are perserved.
  \item \emph{Regular medium-scale spacing.} For each $k$, the values $e_{k,0}, e_{k,1}, \ldots, e_{k, c'\sqrt{n}}$ should span a range of $\Omega(n)$ and increase roughly uniformly, so that $\Theta(\sqrt{n})$ of them are pairwise separated by $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$.
\end{enumerate}

Write $\Delta_{k, i} \coloneqq e_{k, i} - e_{k, i - 1}$ and we have the following lemma, whose first two properties correspond to the first two points above, and the last two properties combined ensures regular medium-scale spacing. 

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:double-exposure}
Let $G$ be a $C$-Ramsey graph, let $\mathbf{S}$, $\mathbf{T}$, $\mathbf{X}$, $U_0, c', d$ be obtained from Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}. Let $U$ be a random subset of $U_0$ where each vertex is present with probability $1/2$, and let $U_{k, i}, e_{k, i}, \Delta_{k, i}$ be defined as above. Then there exist positive constants $M, \beta, Q > 0$ depending on $C$, such that $Q \ge 3\beta$ and for each $c'\sqrt{n} \le k \le 2c'\sqrt{n}$ the following hold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item With probability at least $0.99$, there is a set $\mathcal{I}_k$ of $(1 - \beta/(2M)) \cdot c'\sqrt{n}$ values with the following property: for each $i \in \mathcal{I}_k$, there exists $\mathbf{X}_{k, i} \subseteq \mathbf{X}$ of size $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$, such that the values $d_{U_{k,i}}(\mathbf{x})$ for
        $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}_{k,i}$ are distinct and all lie in the interval $[d/2 - Q\sqrt{n},\; d/2 + Q\sqrt{n}]$.

    \item With probability at least $0.99$,
    \[
      |e_{k,0} - \mathbb{E}[e_{k,0}]| \le Qn.
    \]

    \item With probability at least $1/2$,
    \[
      e_{k,c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k,0} \ge 3\beta n.
    \]

    \item With probability at
        least $0.99$,
        \[
            \sum_{\substack{i \,:\, |\Delta_{k,i}| \ge M\sqrt{n}}} |\Delta_{k,i}| \le \beta n.
        \]
        That is, the unusually large increments contribute little to the total range $e_{k,c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k,0}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  We prove each property in the order of 3, 4, 1, 2, so that the constants $\beta, M, Q$ may be determined in their dependency order.

  \begin{enumerate}
    \item[3.] Note that $|e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})| \leq 2c'\sqrt{n}$, as $|\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}| = k \leq 2c'\sqrt{n}$. This and the construction of $U$ yields
    \begin{align*}
      \E[e_{k, i} - e_{k, i - 1}] 
      &= \E[e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})] + [e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})] \\
      &\geq \frac{1}{2}[e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})] - 2c'\sqrt{n}.
    \end{align*}
    It follows form Property~3 of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} that $e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(U_0, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1}) \geq 8c'\sqrt{n}$, and so 
    \[
      \E[e_{k, i} - e_{k, i - 1}]  \geq 2c'\sqrt{n},
    \]
    for all $i$. Put $\beta = 2(c')^2/3$ so the difference $e_{k, c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k, 0}$ has expectation at least $3\beta n$. But then since $e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) = \sum_{u \in U_0} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} \cdot d_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u)$,
    \begin{align*}
      e_{k, c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k, 0} 
      &= [e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, 0})] + [e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, 0})] \\
      &= [e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, 0})] + \sum_{u \in U_0} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} \cdot (d_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u) - d_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, 0}}(u))
    \end{align*}
    is of Littlewood-Offord type with probability $1/2$, which is symmetrically distributed around its expectation. The result now follows.
    \item[4.] Note that 
    \[
      \Delta_{k, i} = [e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1})] + \sum_{u \in U_0} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} \cdot (d_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u) - d_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1}}(u))
    \]
    is of Littlewood-Offord type and measures the difference of edge count after swapping exactly one vertex from $\mathbf{S}$ to $\mathbf{T}$, so by Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} the expectation of $\Delta_{k, i}$ is $O(\sqrt{n})$ and each $u \in U_0$ contributes at most $|d_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u) - d_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i - 1}}(u)| \leq 2$ to $\Delta_{k, i}$. Thus, the Hoeffding's inequality yields
    \[
      \prob[|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq t] \leq 2\exp\left(\frac{2(t - \E[|\Delta_{k, i}|])^2}{4|U_0|}\right) = \exp(-\Omega(t^2/n)).
    \]
    Using the identity $\E[X] = \sum_{t \geq 1} \prob(X \geq t)$ for any nonnegative integer random variable $X$, we get
    \begin{align*}
      \E\left[|\Delta_{k, i}| \cdot \mathbbm{1}_{|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq M\sqrt{n}}\right]
      &= \sum_{t = 1}^{\infty} \prob\left[|\Delta_{k, i}| \cdot \mathbbm{1}_{|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq M\sqrt{n}} \geq t\right] \\
      &= M\sqrt{n} \cdot \prob\left[|\Delta_{k, i} \geq M\sqrt{n}|\right] + \sum_{t = M\sqrt{n}}^{\infty} \prob\left[|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq t\right] \\
      &= M\sqrt{n} \cdot \exp(-\Omega(M^2)) + \sum_{t = M\sqrt{n}}^{\infty} \exp(-\Omega(t^2/n)) \\
      &= \sqrt{n} \cdot \exp(-\Omega(M^2)),
    \end{align*}
    uniformly over $M$. It now follows from Markov's inequality that, for large enough $M$,
    \[
     \prob\left[\sum_{\substack{i \,:\, |\Delta_{k,i}| \ge M\sqrt{n}}} |\Delta_{k,i}| > \beta n\right] \leq \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathbf{S}|} \E\left[|\Delta_{k, i}| \cdot \mathbbm{1}_{|\Delta_{k, i}| \geq M\sqrt{n}}\right]}{\beta n} \leq \frac{e^{-\Omega(M^2)}}{\beta} < 0.99.
    \]
    \item[1.] For each $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \binom{\mathbf{X}}{2}$, the difference
    \[
      d_{U}(\mathbf{x}) - d_{U}(\mathbf{y}) = \sum_{u \in N_U(\mathbf{x})} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} - \sum_{u \in N_U(\mathbf{y})} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} = \sum_{u \in N_U(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N_U(\mathbf{y})} a_u \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U}
    \]
    with $a_u \in \{1, -1\}$. Thus for each $k, i$, the random variable $d_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x}) - d_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{y})$ is of $(|N_U(\mathbf{x}) \Delta N_U(\mathbf{y})|, 1/2)$-Littlewood-Offord type, as it is simply translation of $d_{U}(\mathbf{x}) - d_{U}(\mathbf{y})$ by some deterministic value. It follows from Property~4 of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} and Littlewood-Offord theorem (Proposition~\ref{prop:littlewood-offord}) that 
    \[
      \prob[d_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x}) = d_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{y})] = O(1/\sqrt{n}).
    \]
    Let $H_{k, i}$ be the graph on $\mathbf{X}$ with edges $\{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\} \in \binom{\mathbf{X}}{2}$ such that $d_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x}) = d_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{y})$, so we have $\E[e(H_{k, i})] = |\mathbf{X}|^2 \cdot O(1/\sqrt{n}) = O(\sqrt{n})$. By Markov's inequality, for large enough $n$,
    \[
      \prob[e(H_{k, i}) = O(\sqrt{n})] \geq 1 - \frac{\E[e(H_{k, i})]}{\omega(\sqrt{n})} \geq 1 - \frac{\beta}{400M},
    \]
    in which case Turán's theorem (Proposition~\ref{prop:turan}) yields an independent set $\mathbf{Y}_{k, i}$ of size $n/(2O(\sqrt{n}) + \sqrt{n}) = 2\gamma \sqrt{n}$, for some constant $\gamma = \gamma(\beta, M) > 0$. In particular, $d_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x})$ is distinct for $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{Y}_{k, i}$. The expected proportion of values of $i$ for which this fails to occur is $\beta/400M$, and by Markov's inequality again, it fails only for $\beta/2M$ proportion of values of $i$ with probability at least $1 - (\beta/400M)/(\beta/2M) = 0.995$, in which case put $\mathcal{I}_k$ as the set of $i$ that succeeds.

    By Property~2 of Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} we have $\E[d_U(\mathbf{x})] = d/2 + O(\sqrt{n})$ for each $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$. Since $d_U(\mathbf{x})$ follows a binomial distribution with parameters $O(n)$ and $1/2$, we also have $\Var(d_U(\mathbf{x})) = O(n)$. Note that by triangle inequality, 
    \[
      |d_U(\mathbf{x}) - d/2| \leq |d_U(\mathbf{x}) - \E[d_U(\mathbf{x})]| + O(\sqrt{n}),
    \]
    and so for sufficiently large $Q$, Chebyshev's inequality yields
    \[
      \prob\left[|d_U(\mathbf{x}) - d/2| > Q\sqrt{n}\right] \leq \frac{\Var(d_U(\mathbf{x}))}{Q^2 n} = \frac{O(1)}{Q^2} < \frac{\gamma}{200}.
    \]
    Let $\mathbf{Y}$ be the set of all $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$ such that $|d_U(\mathbf{x}) - d/2| \leq Q\sqrt{n}$. Then each $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}$ is not present in $\mathbf{Y}$ with probability less than $\gamma/200$. By Markov's inequality,
    \[
      \prob\left[|\mathbf{Y}| \geq (1 - \gamma)|\mathbf{X}|\right] = 1 - \prob\left[|\mathbf{X} \setminus \mathbf{Y}| \geq \gamma|\mathbf{X}|\right] \geq 1 - \frac{\gamma|\mathbf{X}|}{200\gamma |\mathbf{X}|} = 0.995.
    \]
    In other words, with probability at least $0.995$, there exists large enough constant $Q$ and a set $\mathbf{Y} \subseteq \mathbf{X}$ of size at least $(1 - \gamma)|\mathbf{X}|$ whose elements all lie in the interval $[d/2 - Q\sqrt{n},\; d/2 + Q\sqrt{n}]$.

    By the union bound, both above events occur with probability at least $0.99$, in which case we take $\mathbf{X}_{k, i} = \mathbf{Y}_{k, i} \cap \mathbf{Y}$ for $i \in \mathcal{I}_k$. Since $|\mathbf{Y}_{k, i}| \geq 2\gamma \sqrt{n}$ and $|\mathbf{Y}| \geq (1 - \gamma)|\mathbf{X}|$, we have $|\mathbf{X}_{k, i}| \geq \gamma \sqrt{n}$. This proves Property~1.

    \item[2.] Since
    \[
      e_{k, i} = e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) + e(U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) = e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}) + \sum_{u \in U_0} \mathbbm{1}_{u \in U} \cdot d_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u),
    \]
    the random variable $e_{k, i}$ is of $(O(n), 1/2)$-Littlewood-Offord type with coefficients $d_{\mathbf{Z}_{k, i}}(u) = O(\sqrt{n})$. It follows that $\Var(e_{k, 0}) = O(n^2)$ and by Chebyshev's inequality,
    \[
      \prob\left[|e_{k, 0} - \E[e_{k, 0}]| \leq Qn\right] \geq 1 - \frac{\Var(e_{k, 0})}{Q^2 n^2} = 0.99,
    \]
    for large enough $Q$. We end this proof by noting that enlarging $Q$ does not fail Property~1.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of the main lemma}

We now assemble Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} and Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure} to prove the main lemma.

Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:construction} to obtain $U_0, \mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}, c', d$, and define $\mathcal{P}, U, \mathbf{Z}_{k, i}, U_{k, i}$ as in Section~\ref{sec:double-exposure}. Note that $U$ is the only source of randomness. We prove the main lemma with $W$ being the set of all vertices in $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{X}$.

Recall that $k$ ranges over the interval between $c'\sqrt{n}$ and $2c'\sqrt{n}$. For each $k$, union bound shows that all four properties of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure} hold with probability at least $0.4$. Let $\mathcal{K}$ be the set of all such $k$. Then $\E\left[c'\sqrt{n} - |\mathcal{K}|\right] \leq 0.6 \, c'\sqrt{n}$, and Markov's inequality yields
\[
  \prob\left[|\mathcal{K}| \geq 0.2 \, c'\sqrt{n}\right] = 1 - \prob\left[c'\sqrt{n} - |\mathcal{K}| \geq 0.8 \, c'\sqrt{n}\right] \geq 1 - \frac{\E\left[c'\sqrt{n} - |\mathcal{K}|\right]}{0.8 \, c'\sqrt{n}} \geq 0.25.
\]
We now show that $e(U)$ is close to $m$ with high probability. Note that $|m - \E[e(U)]| = O(n^{3/2})$, as $\E[e(U)] = e(U_0)/4$ and $|e(U_0) - 4m| = O(n^{3/2})$ from Lemma~\ref{lem:construction}. Since the presence of edges in $G[U]$ are dependent only if they share a vertex, there are $O(n^3)$ dependent pairs of edges in $G[U]$. Thus we have $\Var(e(U)) \leq O(n^3) + \E[e(U)] = O(n^3)$. Since $|e(U) - m| \leq |e(U) - \E[e(U)]| + |\E[e(U)] - m|$, Chebyshev's inequality yields
\[
  \prob\left[|e(U) - m| = O(n^{3/2})\right] \geq \prob\left[|e(U) - \E[e(U)]| = O(n^{3/2})\right] \geq 1 - \frac{\Var(e(U))}{\omega(n^{3})} \geq 0.9,
\]
for large enough $n$. Fix an outcome $U$ satisfying both events above, which hold together with probability at least $0.25 + 0.9 - 1 = 0.15$. Let $Q, \beta, M, \mathcal{I}_k$ be given by Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure}. 

For each $k$, we have 
\[
  \E[e_{k, 0}] - \E[e_{k - 1, 0}] = \frac{1}{2}[e(\mathbf{Z}_{k, 0}, U_0) - e(\mathbf{Z}_{k - 1, 0}, U_0)] + O(\sqrt{n}) = \Theta(n),
\]
as $\E[d_U(\mathbf{x})] = d_{U_0}(\mathbf{x})/2 = \Theta(n)$ for $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{S}$. We find a subset from $\mathcal{K}$ whose realized values $e_{k, 0}$ are well-separated by a linear gap. Specifically, let $\mathcal{K}' \subseteq \mathcal{K}$ contain every $q$th element of $\mathcal{K}$, for sufficiently large $q$ such that the values of $\E[e_{k, 0}]$ are separated by at least $4Qn$ for $k \in \mathcal{K}'$. Then, $|\mathcal{K}'| = \Theta(\sqrt{n})$ and Property~2 of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure} ensures that the values of $e_{k, 0}$ are separated by at least $2Qn$ for $k \in \mathcal{K}'$. 

We move on to partition the linear gaps between the values of $e_{k, 0} : k \in \mathcal{K}'$ into square-root sized gaps. Let $k \in \mathcal{K}'$. By Property~3 of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure}, we have $e_{k, c'\sqrt{n}} - e_{k, 0} \geq 3\beta n$. Consider the range of integers between $e_{k, 0}$ and $e_{k, 0} + 3\beta n$. Within this range, we take $2\beta\sqrt{n}/M$ intervals of length $M\sqrt{n}$, each separated by a distance of $M\sqrt{n}/2 = \Omega(\sqrt{n})$. By Property~4 of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure}, at most $\beta \sqrt{n} / M$ of these intervals contain no value of $e_{k, i}$. Consider the $\beta\sqrt{n}/M$ intervals that do contain a value of $e_{k, i}$ and pick a representative $e_{k, i}$ from each such interval. Let $\mathcal{I}'_k$ be the set of indices $i$ corresponding to these representatives, and let $\mathcal{I}_k'' = \mathcal{I}_k \cap \mathcal{I}'_k$. Then 
\[
  |\mathcal{I}_k''| = |\mathcal{I}'_k| + |\mathcal{I}'_k| - |\mathcal{I}'_k \cup \mathcal{I}_k| \geq \beta\sqrt{n}/M - \beta c' \sqrt{n}/2M = \Omega(\sqrt{n})
\]
and the values of $e_{k, i}$ are separated by $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$ for $i \in \mathcal{I}_k''$. Recall that $Q \geq 3\beta$, and so by construction $|e_{k, i} - e_{k, 0}| \leq 3\beta n \leq Qn$ for $i \in \mathcal{I}_k''$. In particular, amongst choices of $k \in \mathcal{K}'$ and $\mathcal{I}_k''$, we already have $\Omega(n)$ distinct values of $e(U_{k, i})$ that are separated by $\Omega(\sqrt{n})$. It remains to fill the square-root sized gaps between these values. 

In increasing order of these $e(U_{k, i})$, let $\mathcal{P}' \subseteq \{(k, i) : k \in \mathcal{K}', i \in \mathcal{I}_k''\}$ be the corresponding index set of every $q$th of these values. We enlarge $q$ so that the values $e(U_{k, i})$ from $\mathcal{P}'$ are separated by at least $2Qn$. For each $(k, i) \in \mathcal{P}'$ we have $i \in \mathcal{I}_k$, so Property~1 of Lemma~\ref{lem:double-exposure} furnishes a set $\mathbf{X}_{k, i}$ such that the values $d_{U_{k, i}}(\mathbf{x})$ are distinct for $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{X}_{k, i}$ while lying in a fixed interval of length $2Qn$ centered at $d/2$, where $d$ is independent of $k$ and $i$. In particular, for each $(k, i) \in \mathcal{P}'$, the set
\[
  \{e(U_{k, i} \cup \mathbf{x}_{k, i}) : \mathbf{x}_{k, i} \in \mathbf{X}_{k, i}\}
\]
lie in an interval of length $2Qn$ centered at $e(U_{k, i}) + d/2$, and so these sets do not overlap among different $(k, i) \in \mathcal{P}'$. 

Therefore, 
\[
  |\{e(U \cup Z) : Z \subseteq W\}| \geq |\{e(U_{k, i} \cup \mathbf{x}_{k, i}) : (k, i) \in \mathcal{P}', \, \mathbf{x}_{k, i} \in \mathbf{X}_{k, i}\}| = \Omega(n^{3/2}). 
\]
\qed
